I want to start by addressing a concern that many Singaporeans share: our energy security in a world marked by uncertainty and conflict. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas, making us heavily dependent on supplies from abroad. This reliance exposes us to vulnerabilities—especially when conflicts erupt in critical regions like the Middle East, which can disrupt global energy availability and drive up prices. These disruptions are not remote issues; they directly affect our economy and daily lives here at home.

Having considered these risks, let us now examine how Singapore maintains resilience despite these challenges.

Singapore’s strategy rests on deliberate diversification and strong infrastructure. We import natural gas from several countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, using both pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. This approach reduces dependence on any single supplier or region. In addition, our LNG terminal has a peak send-out capacity of about 11 million tonnes per annum, offering a substantial buffer against short-term supply shocks. Coupled with strategic reserves, these measures provide a robust foundation to manage fluctuations in the global energy market.

Building on this resilience, Singapore is also preparing for a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Looking ahead, we are investing in low-carbon energy sources to strengthen long-term security. By 2035, Singapore aims to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—roughly 30% of our projected demand. A significant initiative supporting this goal is the Lao PDR-Thailand-Malaysia-Singapore Power Integration Project, or LTMS-PIP. This project marks ASEAN’s first multilateral cross-border electricity trade and facilitates imports of renewable energy such as solar power from regional grids. Alongside this effort, we are developing alternatives like hydrogen and advancing energy efficiency across sectors to reduce overall consumption.

While technology and policy play crucial roles, the participation of each one of us is equally important.

Energy security depends not only on government action but also on the choices made by households and businesses. In 2023, households accounted for approximately 8 terawatt hours of electricity consumption—about 14.5% of the national total. This means that responsible energy use at home can significantly contribute to national resilience. By conserving energy and adopting mindful practices, we all help reduce demand during times when supply may be constrained.

Finally, let us consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and affordability.

Our policies must carefully navigate three interconnected priorities: ensuring reliable supply, achieving environmental sustainability, and maintaining affordability for consumers and businesses. For example, Singapore’s carbon tax will increase to S$45 per ton by 2026—a measure designed to encourage emissions reductions while weighing economic impacts carefully. We recognize that balancing these objectives is complex; some may worry about rising costs or potential disruptions as we transition. However, long-term planning remains essential to manage global uncertainties—from geopolitical tensions to technological shifts—and secure an energy future that supports both growth and environmental commitments.

In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security remains strong because of strategic diversification, innovation in low-carbon technologies, resilient infrastructure, active public participation in conservation efforts, and balanced policy planning. It is through this combination that we can confidently address the challenges posed by global disruptions.

Thank you for your attention.
