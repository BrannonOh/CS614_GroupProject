Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was just a distant idea—something we saw in sci-fi or the news. Today, AI is no longer far-off. It’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. We use it when planning holidays—getting personalised recommendations and customised itineraries. In our public service, AI is transforming how we serve you. Our civil service call centres now transcribe conversations in all four official languages—English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil—and generate summaries so officers can focus on helping you directly. This isn’t just convenience; it’s a fundamental change in how government works.

The speed of AI’s progress is striking. In 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems. By 2025, it can compete in the International Math Olympiad—performing at a gold medal level. This shows AI is not some distant theory; it’s a defining technology that’s already reshaping daily life and public services.

As we gather this National Day Rally—our moment to reflect and plan ahead—the question is clear: how do we harness AI to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean?

Artificial intelligence will define this era. Singapore must deploy it across our economy to stay ahead.

We have a unique advantage: combining technology with human expertise attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs here at home. This isn’t just for big companies; small and medium enterprises can benefit too.

Look at Tuas Port. Here, AI works with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations—cutting costs and boosting efficiency in one of the world’s busiest ports. At Changi Airport, AI is being studied carefully for automation that improves efficiency without compromising service quality—a balance crucial for our reputation as a global hub.

GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components early and alert human operators for precise diagnosis. This blend of AI insight and human skill saves time and raises quality.

Even SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay better than before. These are concrete examples of how AI raises productivity across sectors and company sizes.

But whenever technology disrupts industries, the question arises: what will this mean for jobs?

This concern is real—and it deserves an honest answer.

Technological change has always caused disruption. Workers worry about losing jobs or skills becoming obsolete. But history shows us something else: disruption also creates opportunities for workers to move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive—a political decision led by then-Prime Minister Lee Kuan Yew—to equip students and workers with computer skills that transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it decisively under Goh Chok Tong’s leadership, building new industries that propelled Singapore forward.

At PSA, crane operators who once worked physically on-site were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously. This didn’t cut jobs—it upgraded them while boosting productivity and job quality.

Technological shifts are tough—but they don’t have to mean fewer jobs or lower living standards. They can open doors to better roles that pay more.

Just as we supported workers through past transformations—from computers to the internet—we must do so again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will remain people-centred—and clear about what this means.

The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs so workers gain relevant skills for new roles created by AI adoption. This collaboration means unions won’t just be observers—they’ll be partners shaping job redesigns on the ground.

We’re expanding job matching services—holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils to help Singaporeans find good jobs close to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow as well—offering more part-time training allowances and courses from industry leaders and private training providers who know what skills matter today.

This programme already provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months—a significant commitment to mid-career learning.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example—a 41-year-old in 2025 who transitioned from private hire driver to Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS. Vinoth’s story shows what happens when support meets opportunity: real career transformation.

This isn’t about slogans or empty promises—it’s about concrete steps putting people at the centre of technological change.

Ultimately, these efforts serve something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic—a place where good jobs exist and every Singaporean can improve their life through hard work and skill development.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just growth on paper but real improvements on the ground.

Embracing AI supports this goal by helping Singapore stay globally competitive—not by chance but by design—with ideas driving innovation—and unity keeping us strong together.

To those who say technology will leave workers behind or hollow out our economy—I say this: history does not repeat itself if we refuse it permission. We have faced disruption before—and emerged stronger because we planned ahead and put people first through clear policies backed by political will.

This National Day Rally moment calls on us not just to reflect on how far we’ve come—but also on how we move forward together into this new era shaped by artificial intelligence.

Thank you.
