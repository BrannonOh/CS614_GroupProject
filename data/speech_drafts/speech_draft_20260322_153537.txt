Have you ever wondered how Singapore manages to keep its lights on, even when global conflicts erupt far from our shores? This question is more than rhetorical; it underscores a fundamental vulnerability we face as a nation. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas, which means our energy supply is heavily dependent on external sources. Such reliance inherently exposes us to risks—when conflicts arise in key regions, like the Middle East, supply chains can be disrupted. These disruptions not only threaten the stability of our energy supply but also cause significant price volatility, impacting both our economy and daily lives. Understanding this vulnerability is crucial as we consider how Singapore maintains energy security amid such global uncertainties. Having established these risks, let us now explore how Singapore stays resilient despite these challenges.

To address this vulnerability, Singapore has developed a comprehensive strategy focused on diversification and robust infrastructure. Our natural gas imports come from multiple countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, delivered via pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. Beyond piped gas, we secure supplies from major LNG exporters such as Australia, Qatar, and the United States. This deliberate diversification reduces dependency on any single source or region, thereby strengthening supply security. In addition to diversifying suppliers, Singapore has invested in strong infrastructure: our LNG terminal has a peak send-out capacity of approximately 11 million tonnes per annum. Complementing this capacity are strategic energy stockpiles maintained to cover several months of demand. Together, these measures provide essential buffers against short-term shocks and potential supply interruptions. With these strategies firmly in place, Singapore is also looking ahead to secure its energy future sustainably.

Looking forward, Singapore is actively pursuing renewable and low-carbon initiatives to enhance energy security while addressing environmental imperatives. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—about 30% of our projected demand—from regional sources. This includes concrete plans such as importing 1 gigawatt of hydropower from Sarawak in Malaysia and solar energy from interconnected grids within the region. Concurrently, efforts are underway to develop low-carbon alternatives like hydrogen; for example, Keppel is constructing a 600-megawatt hydrogen-ready power plant aligned with Singapore’s National Hydrogen Strategy. Improving energy efficiency remains a core priority alongside these initiatives. These tangible projects represent strategic steps toward building a more resilient and sustainable energy system for the nation. Yet it is important to recognize that ensuring energy security depends not only on infrastructure and technology but also on the collective responsibility of every individual.

Energy security extends beyond government policy—it requires active participation from households and businesses alike. In Singapore, households account for about 15% of total electricity consumption; thus, conservation efforts at home can meaningfully contribute to national resilience. Building awareness around responsible energy use strengthens our collective ability to withstand external shocks. National campaigns such as the Go25 movement encourage citizens to set air-conditioners at 25 degrees Celsius—a practical step toward reducing consumption without compromising comfort. These initiatives demonstrate how individual actions directly support broader strategies in maintaining our energy security. Finally, let us consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and cost.

Singapore faces the complex challenge of balancing three interrelated priorities: reliable energy supply, environmental sustainability, and affordability—often referred to as the energy trilemma. Our policies must carefully navigate these sometimes competing objectives to ensure long-term stability and progress. Since 2019, Singapore has implemented a carbon tax that provides an economic signal encouraging businesses and consumers to reduce carbon-intensive activities while supporting sustainable growth. Long-term planning remains essential so that even amid global uncertainties, Singapore can maintain secure access to affordable and clean energy resources. In conclusion, Singapore maintains its energy security through strategic diversification of sources and suppliers, innovation in low-carbon technologies, and shared responsibility across society—ensuring that we remain resilient now and into the future.
