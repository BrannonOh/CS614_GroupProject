Good evening, ladies and gentlemen.

Artificial intelligence, or AI, has become one of the most widely discussed technologies in recent years. Across many industries and countries, AI is often described as a tool that could reshape how economies function and how organizations operate. Singapore is no exception to this trend. As a nation that has long relied on innovation and efficiency to remain competitive, Singapore must also consider carefully how artificial intelligence fits into our broader economic future.

In many ways, AI represents the next stage of technological development. Over the past few decades, we have seen major waves of technological change. The spread of computers transformed office work. The internet connected people and businesses around the world. Mobile technology changed how we communicate and consume information. Artificial intelligence now builds on these developments by allowing machines to analyze data, detect patterns, and assist humans in making decisions.

Today, AI is already present in many aspects of daily life. Many people interact with AI systems without even realizing it. For example, travel applications use algorithms to recommend destinations and create personalized travel plans. Online platforms use AI systems to suggest products, services, or information based on previous behavior. Even public services are beginning to adopt AI technologies to improve efficiency and reduce administrative workload.

Singapore’s public sector has explored ways to incorporate AI into certain operational processes. For instance, call centres in government agencies may use automated tools to transcribe conversations and generate summaries for officers. This can allow officers to focus more on responding to citizens’ needs instead of spending time on manual documentation. These types of systems illustrate how AI can serve as a supporting tool rather than replacing human decision-making.

At the same time, businesses are also experimenting with AI applications. In large infrastructure environments such as ports and airports, automation and machine learning technologies are being studied to improve logistics operations. Artificial intelligence systems can help analyze large volumes of operational data and detect potential inefficiencies or anomalies. In some industrial settings, AI tools may help engineers identify problems with machinery before those problems lead to major failures.

These examples demonstrate that artificial intelligence has the potential to improve productivity in different sectors of the economy. However, while productivity gains are often highlighted in discussions about AI, the human implications of technological change must also be considered.

One of the most common concerns about artificial intelligence relates to employment. When new technologies emerge, workers may worry that machines will eventually replace certain types of jobs. These concerns are understandable. Throughout history, technological shifts have sometimes disrupted existing industries and changed the nature of work.

However, historical experience also suggests that technological progress does not necessarily lead to permanent job losses. Instead, it often changes the types of skills that are required in the workforce. In the past, Singapore has navigated several waves of technological change by investing in education, training, and workforce development.

For example, during earlier periods of economic modernization, the government introduced programs to improve digital literacy and technical capabilities among workers. When computers became widely adopted, many workers had to learn new software systems and digital processes. Similarly, when the internet became more integrated into business operations, organizations began to rely on new types of technical expertise.

Artificial intelligence may represent another similar transition. Rather than eliminating the need for workers entirely, AI may shift the kinds of tasks that workers perform. Certain repetitive or data-intensive tasks may become more automated, while human workers focus more on supervision, analysis, and decision-making.

In some industries, this transition is already visible. For instance, technological improvements in port operations have allowed certain equipment to be operated remotely. Workers who previously performed physically demanding tasks on-site may now manage operations through digital interfaces. While this requires new skills, it can also create safer and more technically sophisticated job roles.

Despite these possibilities, adapting to technological change can still be difficult for many individuals. Workers who have spent many years in a particular occupation may find it challenging to transition into a different type of role. This is why workforce development programs remain an important part of Singapore’s strategy.

Singapore has introduced several initiatives designed to support skills upgrading and career mobility. One well-known initiative is the SkillsFuture program, which provides credits that individuals can use to pursue training courses and professional development opportunities. The objective of these programs is to encourage continuous learning and help workers remain adaptable in a changing economic environment.

More recently, additional initiatives have been introduced to support mid-career workers. For example, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides additional financial support for Singaporeans aged forty and above who wish to pursue further education or professional training. These types of policies aim to ensure that workers are not left behind as industries evolve.

However, training programs alone are not enough. Successful workforce adaptation also depends on collaboration between different stakeholders. Governments, businesses, unions, and educational institutions all play roles in shaping the future workforce.

Companies must be willing to invest in employee development and redesign jobs so that workers can work effectively alongside new technologies. Educational institutions must ensure that their programs remain relevant to evolving industry needs. Unions can also help represent workers’ concerns and ensure that transitions are managed responsibly.

In addition, job matching initiatives and career support services can help individuals identify new opportunities. Career fairs, employment support centers, and digital job platforms can connect workers with employers who are seeking particular skills.

Ultimately, the goal of these efforts is to maintain an economy that remains both dynamic and inclusive. Technological progress should not benefit only a small group of individuals or companies. Instead, it should contribute to broader economic resilience and social stability.

Artificial intelligence has the potential to play an important role in this process. If used responsibly, AI can help organizations operate more efficiently and enable workers to focus on higher-value tasks. At the same time, policies and institutions must ensure that workers have access to opportunities for retraining and career advancement.

Looking ahead, Singapore will likely continue exploring how artificial intelligence can support economic growth and national competitiveness. However, it is important to approach this transition carefully and thoughtfully.

Technological innovation should always be balanced with long-term considerations about social impact and workforce sustainability. By maintaining a focus on skills development, collaboration, and responsible adoption, Singapore can navigate the opportunities and challenges associated with artificial intelligence.

Artificial intelligence is not a solution to every problem, but it is an important tool that will influence how economies evolve in the coming years. Singapore’s ability to adapt, invest in its people, and manage technological change will determine how successfully we harness these developments.

Thank you.
