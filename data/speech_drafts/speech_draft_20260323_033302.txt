Let’s start with something striking. Just a few months ago, early 2024, AI models were wrestling with grade-school math problems. Fast forward to today, and these same models are winning gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad—a competition that demands top-tier problem-solving skills. That’s not just progress; it’s a leap. Here in Singapore, AI isn’t some distant concept; it’s already woven into our daily lives. Our civil service call centres use AI to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and whip up summaries for officers. And it’s not just government work—according to a recent Booking.com study, over half of Singaporean travellers—55%—are keen to use AI tools when planning their trips. From classrooms to airports, from government offices to your holiday plans, AI has transformed dramatically in just a few years. It’s no longer limited or experimental; it’s embedded in everyday activities and public services right here at home. This rapid evolution and integration of AI opens new possibilities for Singapore’s industries and workforce, which we will explore next.

Now, what does this mean for our economy and jobs? Singapore is blending technology with human expertise like no other place. Take Tuas Port: AI works hand-in-hand with robotics and autonomous machines to boost efficiency. Over at Changi Airport, they’re studying automation powered by AI to streamline operations. GE Vernova uses AI here in Singapore to detect turbine anomalies early and support human operators in making faster decisions. And it’s not just big players—SMEs like Q&M Dental rely on AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately than ever before. This isn’t about replacing people; it’s about working smarter together. The government is actively pushing this transformation through strategic programs and partnerships with global tech leaders, aiming to attract investments and create quality jobs across the board—from large corporations to small businesses. The result? Higher productivity and stronger competitiveness on the world stage. But let’s be honest: while AI drives innovation and growth, it also raises real concerns about job displacement—and we need to address those head-on.

Here’s the hard truth: technological change always shakes things up—and that can be scary. But history shows us that it doesn’t have to mean losing out. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a computerisation drive that equipped workers with new skills and fundamentally transformed our economy for the better. At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs—they were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes at once, boosting both productivity and job quality. The Civil Service Computerisation Programme automated routine tasks but also laid the groundwork for e-services like TradeNet that we all rely on today. So yes, disruption happens—but it opens doors too. The focus now is not on replacing jobs but redesigning them—using AI to augment human capabilities and create higher-value roles that pay better and demand more skill. That means a mindset shift: continuous learning and upskilling aren’t optional anymore—they’re essential if we want to stay relevant and grab the opportunities AI creates. Recognizing these lessons, Singapore is committed to ensuring its people are prepared and prioritized in the AI transition.

And how are we doing that? Through concrete action—not just talk. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above a $4,000 credit top-up plus a training allowance of up to $3,000 a month for full-time study—a serious investment in mid-career training. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: he went from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer by tapping into SkillsFuture credits and further studies at SUSS—a real-life example of how these programmes work on the ground. Meanwhile, Workforce Singapore (WSG) alongside its partners placed over 56,000 locals into jobs last fiscal year through expanded job matching services and career fairs designed specifically for Singaporeans navigating this changing landscape. The government is working closely with unions too—to redesign jobs so workers get equipped with the right skills for tomorrow’s economy. These efforts ensure Singaporeans remain at the heart of the nation’s AI-driven future, leading us to the vision ahead.

Looking forward—Singapore isn’t just adapting; we’re setting ambitious goals under our National AI Strategy 2.0 aiming for AI to contribute roughly US$29.6 billion to our economy by 2030—that’s no small change. A recent Salesforce study ranked us second globally—and first in Asia-Pacific—for AI readiness, putting us ahead of many bigger economies out there. And in 2025 alone, more than 105,000 people took up SkillsFuture-supported AI-related training courses—a clear sign that we’re building an army ready for this new era. A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to keep our edge on the global stage—it’s essential. But beyond technology or dollars lies what truly makes us strong: our ideas, our innovation—and most importantly—our unity as one nation moving forward together.

Thank you.
