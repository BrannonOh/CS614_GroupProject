Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was something we only saw in sci-fi movies or read about in tech journals. Today, it’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. We use AI when planning holidays—getting personalised travel recommendations and itineraries tailored just for us. In our public services, AI listens in on calls across all four official languages, transcribing conversations and summarising them so officers can spend more time helping people. This isn’t some far-off future; it’s happening now. And as we gather this National Day Rally, reflecting on where we are and where we’re headed, it’s clear that AI matters to every Singaporean—not just today, but for the future.

Artificial intelligence is shaping up to be the defining technology of our era. Back in 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and it can compete at the International Math Olympiad level—winning gold medals. That kind of leap shows how fast this technology is advancing. So the real question for Singapore isn’t whether AI will affect us—it already has—but how we harness it to boost our productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

Let’s talk about how we put AI to work across the economy. Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise—that’s what attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs here. Take Tuas Port: they’re using AI alongside robotics and autonomous machines to handle operations more efficiently. Over at Changi Airport, they’re studying automation possibilities with AI too. It’s not just the big players; small and medium enterprises are jumping on board as well. For example, GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts and alert operators for precise diagnoses—saving time and improving accuracy. Even smaller companies like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately than before—helping dentists detect fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay faster.

AI adoption isn’t just for large corporations; SMEs can benefit too.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

We get that concern—it’s valid. History shows us that technological change disrupts work but also creates new opportunities and better jobs over time. Look back to the 1980s when Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive: students and workers learned new computer skills that transformed the economy. When the internet came along in the 1990s, Singapore embraced it fully—and that propelled us forward again.

At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to supervise multiple cranes remotely—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Technological change shakes things up but also opens doors to higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

This time around, our approach remains people-centred. The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills needed for this shift. We’re expanding job matching services and holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions so young Singaporeans can connect with new opportunities early on. There’s even a new town-level job matching initiative led by CDCs to reach people closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is growing too—it now covers more part-time training allowance claims and includes courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

For those aged 40 and above, SkillsFuture credits provide up to $4,000 plus monthly training allowances of up to $3,000 for full-time study lasting up to two years.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: at 41 years old in 2025, he shifted careers from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he continues studying Business Analytics at SUSS.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.
