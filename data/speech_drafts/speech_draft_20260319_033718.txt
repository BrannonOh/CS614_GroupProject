Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was something we only heard about in science fiction or distant tech reports. Today, it’s all around us. It’s in the apps we use to plan our holidays—recommending destinations, creating personalised itineraries tailored just for us. It’s in the services we rely on daily. In our civil service, AI helps transcribe calls in all four official languages and generates summaries, freeing up officers to focus on helping the public more effectively. This is not some far-off future; it’s here, now, shaping how we live and work in Singapore.

Just consider how fast AI has progressed: In 2020, it struggled with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete—and win gold medal-level awards—at the International Math Olympiad. That’s a leap that signals how quickly this technology is advancing.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

Artificial intelligence will be a defining technology of our era. If we get this right, it can raise productivity, boost competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean.

Look at Tuas Port: AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, automation powered by AI is being studied to improve efficiency. Even at GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here, AI detects anomalies in turbine parts and alerts human operators for precise diagnosis—combining machine precision with human expertise.

And this isn’t just for big players. Small and medium enterprises are jumping on board too. Take Q&M Dental: they use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, tooth decay—helping dentists make better diagnoses.

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human skills. That mix attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs—not just any jobs, but meaningful ones that build careers.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

We won’t pretend there aren’t challenges ahead. Technological change always disrupts existing roles. But history shows us something important: new technology doesn’t just take away jobs—it creates new ones that are better skilled and better paid.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with essential computer skills. That effort transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate—we adopted it quickly and propelled ourselves forward.

At PSA today, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity and improving job quality at the same time.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will be people-centred from start to finish.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs so workers can thrive alongside new technologies—and not be left behind.

We’re expanding job matching services across the board: more career fairs at tertiary institutions; a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils; all designed to help Singaporeans find opportunities that fit their skills and aspirations.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—more part-time training allowances available; more courses from industry leaders and private training providers included.

This programme already provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for up to two years of full-time study.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: at 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs—and better lives—for all of us.

Embracing AI supports our goal of staying globally competitive—not by chasing trends blindly—but by focusing on ideas, innovation, and unity as a nation.

Singapore aims not just to keep pace but to stay ahead through hard work and smart choices made together.

Thank you.
