Have you ever wondered how secure Singapore’s energy supply truly is, especially in the context of ongoing global conflicts? This question is critical, given that Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas. Our heavy reliance on imported energy, particularly natural gas, exposes us to significant risks. For example, the recent conflicts in the Middle East have driven global liquefied natural gas (LNG) prices upward, which has directly increased electricity costs here at home. Such geopolitical tensions underscore our vulnerability and the potential impact on both our economy and households. Having established this vulnerability, let us now examine how Singapore maintains resilience despite these challenges.

Singapore’s strategy to mitigate energy supply risks is founded on deliberate diversification and robust infrastructure development. We import natural gas from multiple countries—including Indonesia and Malaysia—via pipelines and LNG shipments. In addition to piped gas, our LNG imports are diversified, with Australia serving as a major supplier among others. This diversification effectively reduces dependency on any single region, thereby lowering supply risk. Complementing this approach is our strong infrastructure: the planned second LNG terminal will enhance capacity and provide a critical buffer against short-term supply shocks. These measures collectively ensure that disruptions elsewhere do not compromise Singapore’s energy stability. Looking ahead, Singapore is also transitioning towards a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Central to this transition is Singapore’s commitment to importing low-carbon electricity and developing alternative energy sources to enhance future security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30% of projected demand—through large-scale renewable projects such as the Australia-Asia PowerLink and bilateral agreements with countries including Indonesia and Vietnam. Alongside these imports, Singapore has established a National Hydrogen Strategy and is investing substantially in research and development to integrate hydrogen as a key component of our future energy mix. Furthermore, ongoing efforts to improve energy efficiency across sectors complement these initiatives by reducing overall demand growth. Together, these strategies position Singapore not only to meet increasing energy needs but also to reduce carbon emissions responsibly. But it is important to recognize that energy security depends not only on infrastructure and technology; it also hinges on the actions of individuals and society.

Public participation plays a vital role in strengthening our national resilience. In 2023, households accounted for approximately 14.5% of Singapore’s total electricity consumption—a significant proportion that highlights the impact of everyday energy use on national demand. Energy security is therefore a shared responsibility involving both households and businesses through conscious conservation efforts. National campaigns such as Go Green SG actively promote awareness of responsible energy usage and encourage practices that reduce peak demand and overall consumption. By fostering such collective action, these initiatives help alleviate pressure on supply systems during critical periods and enhance our overall resilience. Finally, we must consider how Singapore balances security, sustainability, and cost within its energy policies.

This balance—commonly referred to as the “energy trilemma”—presents a complex policy challenge: ensuring reliable energy supply while advancing environmental objectives and maintaining affordability for consumers. Singapore’s carbon tax policy exemplifies this tension; increases designed to promote sustainability are projected to raise household utility bills in the near term. Navigating these competing priorities demands careful long-term planning amid persistent global uncertainties related to market dynamics and geopolitical developments. Only through sustained strategic commitment can we preserve energy security without compromising economic competitiveness or environmental responsibilities. In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security remains a dynamic achievement requiring strategic foresight, technological innovation, and collective effort.

To summarize: Singapore ensures its energy security through strategic diversification of supply sources, investment in innovative low-carbon technologies, development of resilient infrastructure, and active public participation in conservation efforts. Every individual has an essential role in sustaining this security for the nation’s future; together, we must remain informed and engaged as we navigate an evolving global landscape with prudence and resolve.
