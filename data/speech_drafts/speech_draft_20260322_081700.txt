Good morning, everyone.

Think back just a few years. AI was this niche thing — something tech geeks talked about but most of us barely saw in daily life. Now? It’s everywhere, deeply embedded in how we live right here in Singapore. And it’s not just small stuff. Take Google DeepMind’s AlphaGeometry: this AI can solve geometry problems at the International Mathematical Olympiad level. That’s no ordinary calculator — it’s tackling some of the toughest math out there.

But it’s not just academic feats. A recent study shows that among Singaporean travelers using Generative AI for planning trips, 92% say it saves them time and makes putting together itineraries effortless. And right here at home, our civil service uses AI in call centres to transcribe conversations in all four official languages — English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil — then generates summaries to help officers respond faster and better.

AI has transformed dramatically in just a few years, becoming a powerful tool woven into many parts of our lives. Singaporeans already interact with AI when booking trips or dealing with government services without even realizing it.

This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and innovation across Singapore’s economy.

Let’s get concrete about what that means on the ground.

AI isn’t reserved for big companies or flashy startups. It’s changing industries across the board — benefiting large enterprises and small businesses alike.

At Tuas Port, AI combined with robotics and autonomous machines is already streamlining operations. Meanwhile, Changi Airport is actively studying automation options to improve efficiency. Over at GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility, AI detects anomalies early and helps human operators diagnose problems more accurately and quickly. Even smaller players like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyze dental X-rays for more precise detection of issues.

Singapore’s unique mix of advanced technology and skilled human expertise doesn’t just attract investments; it creates quality jobs across sectors. The government drives this adoption actively through national strategies and programs designed to support all businesses — including SMEs — ensuring no one gets left behind.

But let me be blunt: this progress raises serious concerns about job displacement.

Technological change doesn’t just shake things up mildly; it disrupts jobs hard, creating real anxiety for workers facing an uncertain future. Look back at history: in 1981, Singapore launched the National Computerisation Plan to computerise the civil service, train software professionals, and build our IT industry from scratch. That was a massive shift.

In the late 1990s, internet adoption surged from under 14% in 1997 to 36% by 2000 — a rapid change that reshaped how we work and live. At PSA, crane operators weren’t pushed out; they were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes simultaneously. This not only boosted productivity but also improved job quality by giving operators more control and reducing physical strain.

These transitions were tough—real disruption and anxiety hit workers hard as they had to adapt to new realities—but they also opened doors to better opportunities and new industries we couldn’t have imagined before.

Building on that history, Singapore is prioritizing its people as we move into the AI era — with targeted support and training designed to help workers thrive amid change.

For example, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above up to $4,000 in credits plus a training allowance for full-time study—equivalent to half their average income capped at $3,000 per month—making mid-career reskilling financially viable.

NTUC's Company Training Committees co-fund up to 70% of companies’ transformation projects; notably, AI-related projects more than doubled in 2025 compared to 2024—a clear sign businesses are stepping up their game with government backing.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: he moved from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer by using SkillsFuture credits combined with further studies at SUSS—a real example of how these programs work on the ground.

The government also collaborates closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for the AI era. Expanded job matching services and career fairs are specifically aimed at helping Singaporeans access new opportunities quickly—making sure no one is left stranded as industries evolve.

These efforts ensure Singaporeans remain at the heart of progress as our nation embraces AI for a dynamic future.

Now ask yourself: can we afford not to embrace AI? The facts are clear.

A Google-commissioned report estimates that AI-powered solutions could add S$198.3 billion—that's US$146 billion—to Singapore's economy by 2030. That’s not small change; it’s transformative economic value on the line.

Globally, Singapore ranks second on the AI Readiness Index because we excel in regulatory readiness, widespread AI adoption across sectors, and an investment environment that attracts cutting-edge innovation.

Prime Minister Lawrence Wong puts it plainly: this is a “People-First AI Vision.” Success won’t come just from data or computing power but from our people—their ideas, their innovation—and crucially, their unity.

Singapore's future depends on these elements coming together strongly—not just innovation alone but innovation backed by everyone pulling in the same direction.

So here’s my challenge: Are we ready to face this head-on? To harness AI thoughtfully and inclusively so we don’t just keep up but lead?

Artificial intelligence is no longer optional or distant—it’s here now. How well we manage it will decide if Singapore stays competitive globally while creating meaningful opportunities for every one of us.

Thank you.
