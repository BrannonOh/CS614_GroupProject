Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Artificial intelligence—AI—is no longer some far-off idea. It’s not just a future possibility. It’s a defining technology of our time, and it’s advancing faster than most of us realize. Just think: in 2020, AI struggled to solve basic math problems reliably. Five years later, in 2025, it can compete in the International Math Olympiad—and perform at a gold medal level. That’s not gradual progress; that’s a leap.

Here in Singapore, AI is already part of everyday life. Many of us use AI for travel planning—where it recommends destinations and generates personalised itineraries tailored to our preferences. In the civil service, AI works quietly but effectively: agency call centres use it to transcribe conversations across all four official languages and produce summaries. This frees up officers to focus on serving you better and faster.

This rapid growth matters now more than ever—especially as we gather at this National Day Rally, a moment to reflect on how far we’ve come and where we’re headed next.

Artificial intelligence will shape our era. Singapore must harness it—to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean.

So AI is no longer distant or theoretical. The real question is: how do we use AI to boost our economy and stay competitive in a world that’s changing faster than ever?

Singapore has always excelled at combining technology with human expertise. This unique mix attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs for our people. Take Tuas Port: AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to speed up operations and push efficiency to new levels. At Changi Airport, we’re actively exploring how AI-driven automation can make processes smoother and smarter.

In industry, GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components early on. When the system flags an issue, human operators step in for precise diagnosis and repair—combining machine speed with human judgment.

And it’s not just big companies benefiting from AI adoption. Small and medium enterprises are tapping into this technology too. For example, Q&M Dental uses AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—helping detect fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay that might otherwise be missed.

This shows something crucial: adopting AI isn’t just for the giants; SMEs can gain real advantages as well.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, there’s a question on everyone’s mind: what will this mean for jobs?

We’ve been here before—and yes, technological change disrupts lives sometimes—but history also tells us something important: it creates new opportunities and better jobs.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—a bold move that transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate; we adopted it quickly and used it as a springboard for growth.

Look at PSA today: crane operators who once worked physically on-site are now retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once. This shift doesn’t just boost productivity—it improves job quality by reducing physical strain while increasing responsibility and oversight.

Technological change has always disrupted—but it also lifts workers into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

That’s the reality we face again with artificial intelligence. And just as before, we must support workers through this transformation.

Our approach will be people-centred from start to finish.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs around AI adoption—and equip workers with the skills they need to stay relevant in this new landscape. We’re expanding job matching services—holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils (CDCs) to connect Singaporeans with opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowance claims alongside an expanded range of courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

To put this into perspective: the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months—a significant support package designed specifically around challenges posed by new technologies like AI.

Consider Vinoth Nanda Kumaran’s story. At 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer. He used SkillsFuture credits first for a diploma in data engineering and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS—all while working towards better prospects made possible by these programmes.

This is concrete proof that targeted support can help Singaporeans seize new opportunities created by technological change.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs—and better lives—for all of us—not just some of us.

Embracing AI is not optional if we want to stay globally competitive; it’s essential. But beyond competition—it’s about staying ahead through ideas, innovation—and unity among all Singaporeans as we face these changes together.

Some say AI will take away jobs or leave people behind. I say this: if we don’t confront these challenges head-on—if we leave how AI fits into our economy up to chance—we risk falling behind instead.

AI is not just technology; it shapes our future together as a nation ready to meet change—not shy away from it.

Thank you.
