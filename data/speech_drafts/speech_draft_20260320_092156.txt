Ladies and gentlemen,

Just a few years ago, artificial intelligence was something we heard about mostly in labs or science fiction. Today, it’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. In 2025, AI models from Google and OpenAI didn’t just make headlines—they won gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s a leap that shows how fast AI has evolved. But it’s not just about impressive feats on the global stage. Right now, Singaporeans use AI to plan their travels with personalized recommendations, making trips smoother and more enjoyable. Our civil service is also tapping into AI—call centres transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generate summaries that help officers serve you better. AI has transformed dramatically in just a few years, becoming something we live with every day.

This rapid progress shows AI is no longer a distant future but a present reality, prompting us to explore how Singapore can harness it for growth.

Across our economy, AI is already making a difference. At Tuas Port, robotics and autonomous machines powered by AI are changing how goods move efficiently. At Changi Airport, AI is being studied to automate key operations. GE Vernova uses AI to detect turbine problems early so repairs are precise and timely—saving costs and boosting reliability. Even small businesses benefit: Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays for better diagnosis and care. This isn’t just about technology; it’s about combining human expertise with smart tools to attract investments and create good jobs across industries—from large corporations to SMEs.

Singapore has backed this up with serious commitment—over S$1 billion invested in public AI research, plus private funding that helped us capture 68% of ASEAN’s AI deal value in the first half of 2024 alone.

Understanding these applications reveals AI’s tangible benefits, but also raises questions about its impact on jobs and workers.

Let’s be clear: technological change disrupts jobs—that’s nothing new. In the 1980s, Singapore’s push for computerisation equipped workers with new skills and transformed our economy. The internet boom in the 1990s propelled us further ahead. At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes simultaneously—improving productivity and job quality at once. Experts today point out that roles like data entry, administrative support, and basic coding are most vulnerable to AI disruption here in Singapore.

But history tells us something important: disruption comes with opportunity. Workers don’t just lose jobs—they move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

This history of adaptation sets the foundation for how Singapore can navigate the AI transition while prioritizing its people.

The government isn’t standing still either. We’re working closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills for this changing landscape. Programs like SkillsFuture Level-Up offer up to $4,000 in credits plus $3,000 monthly training allowances for full-time study—especially targeting Singaporeans aged 40 and above who want to level up their skills. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: he moved from being a driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer by using SkillsFuture credits and further studies—a real-life story of transformation through opportunity.

In 2025 alone, more than 105,000 people took part in SkillsFuture-supported AI training programs—a clear sign that Singaporeans are stepping up to meet this challenge head-on.

Efforts go beyond training too: expanded job matching services, career fairs tailored for emerging industries, even initiatives at the town level make sure no one gets left behind.

These initiatives demonstrate Singapore’s commitment to ensuring that AI benefits all Singaporeans as the economy evolves.

Looking ahead, Singapore’s updated National Artificial Intelligence Strategy—NAIS 2.0—is designed not just to keep pace but to lead. It aims to develop peaks of excellence in AI while empowering people and businesses alike to confidently adopt this technology.

The results speak for themselves: driven by booming demand for AI-related products and services worldwide, we’ve upgraded our economic growth forecast for 2026 to between 2.0 and 4.0 percent.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans.

Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay globally competitive; it’s essential.

Singapore aims to lead through ideas, innovation—and unity.

Thank you.
