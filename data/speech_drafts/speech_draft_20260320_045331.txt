Good evening, everyone.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—seemed like a distant idea, something far off in the future. Today in Singapore, it’s part of our daily lives. We use AI when planning trips—getting personalised recommendations on where to go and what to do. In our civil service, AI transcribes calls in all four official languages and generates summaries, freeing officers to focus on serving you better. Beyond call centres, AI is transforming public services across the board, making them more efficient and responsive. As we gather at this National Day Rally—a moment for reflection and discussing progress—it’s clear: AI matters to every Singaporean now and for the future we want to build.

Artificial intelligence will define our era. For Singapore, it’s not enough to keep pace. We must harness AI to raise productivity, strengthen competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

Consider this leap: in 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems. By 2025, it can compete at the International Math Olympiad level—winning gold medals. That’s rapid progress. Here at home, AI is already woven into how we live and work—from travel planning apps to government services that serve you more effectively.

So AI is no longer distant or theoretical. The real question: how do we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world?

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise. This unique mix attracts high-quality investments that create good jobs for our people. And AI adoption isn’t just for big firms with deep pockets; small and medium enterprises—the backbone of our economy—can benefit too.

Take Tuas Port: AI works hand-in-hand with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, automation is being actively studied to improve efficiency and service quality. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect tiny anomalies in turbine parts—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses. And SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings or tooth decay that might otherwise be missed.

These examples show how AI raises productivity and creates value across industries big and small.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask: what will this mean for jobs?

This concern is valid—and not new. Technological change disrupts work but also creates new opportunities and better jobs.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy over that decade. Then in the 1990s, when the internet arrived, Singapore adopted it successfully—and propelled the nation forward.

At PSA ports today, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

History reassures us: technology shifts work toward higher-skilled roles with better pay; it doesn’t eliminate opportunity.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do so again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will remain people-centred. The government will work closely with NTUC and unions—not just alongside them—to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills fit for an AI-driven economy.

We’re expanding job matching services and holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions. A new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils will bring opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowances and courses from industry leaders and private providers.

To illustrate: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was a private hire driver until recently. At age 41 in 2025, he used his SkillsFuture credits to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. Today he works as a Business Intelligence Developer—a career upgrade made possible by these programmes.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to 24 months of full-time study.

Ultimately, these efforts are about building an economy that stays dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just some or a privileged few.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by blindly chasing trends but by leading through ideas grounded in real skills.

Singapore aims not only to keep pace but to stay ahead—through unity as much as ingenuity.

Now some say AI will replace workers wholesale—that machines will take over completely. But history tells a different story: technology changes how we work but doesn’t erase the need for people who can adapt and learn new skills. We face a choice: harness AI wisely or risk falling behind countries that do.

So at this National Day Rally moment—a time for honest reflection—we must confront this head-on: artificial intelligence isn’t coming tomorrow; it’s here today. The question isn’t if we adopt it—but how we adopt it so every Singaporean benefits from progress.

Let me put it plainly: if we fail to act decisively on AI now, we risk losing ground economically—and with it, opportunities for our people.

But if we get this right—if government agencies collaborate closely with unions; if workers seize chances to upgrade skills; if businesses innovate responsibly—we can shape an economy where technology empowers everyone.

Think of AI as a tool—a powerful one—that can either widen divides or narrow them depending on how we use it.

Our task is clear: make sure it’s the latter—for every worker, every family, every community across Singapore.

Thank you.
