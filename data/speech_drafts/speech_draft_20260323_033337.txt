Let’s get straight to it. Just a few months ago, in early 2024, AI models were struggling with grade-school math problems. Now? They’re winning gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s not just progress—it’s a leap that shows how fast AI has evolved. Here in Singapore, AI isn’t some distant idea anymore. It’s already part of our daily lives. Our civil service call centres use AI to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generate summaries for officers. And it’s not just government work—according to a Booking.com study, over half of Singaporean travellers—55%—are ready to use AI tools when planning their trips. In just a few years, AI has gone from limited capabilities to high-level performance, embedded in everyday activities and public services right here at home. This rapid evolution and integration of AI opens new possibilities for Singapore’s industries and workforce, which we will explore next.

So, what does this mean for our economy and jobs? Singapore is combining technology with human expertise like no other place. Look at Tuas Port: AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to boost efficiency. At Changi Airport, they’re testing AI-driven automation to streamline operations. GE Vernova uses AI here to detect turbine problems early and help human operators make faster decisions. And it’s not just big companies—SMEs like Q&M Dental rely on AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately than ever before. This isn’t about replacing people; it’s about working smarter together. The government is pushing this hard through strategic programmes and partnerships with global tech leaders, aiming to attract investments and create quality jobs—from large corporations down to small businesses. The result? Higher productivity and stronger competitiveness on the world stage. But let’s be clear: while AI drives growth and innovation, it also raises real concerns about job losses—and we need to face those head-on.

Here’s the blunt truth: technological change always shakes things up—and that can be uncomfortable or even scary. But history shows us it doesn’t have to mean losing out. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a computerisation drive that equipped workers with new skills and transformed our economy for the better. At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs—they were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes at once, boosting productivity and improving job quality. The 1980s Civil Service Computerisation Programme automated routine tasks but also laid the groundwork for e-services like TradeNet that we all rely on today. Disruption happens—but it opens doors too. The focus now is not on replacing jobs but redesigning them—using AI to augment human capabilities and create higher-value roles that pay better and demand more skill. That means a mindset shift: continuous learning and upskilling aren’t optional—they’re essential if we want to stay relevant and seize the opportunities AI creates. Recognizing these lessons, Singapore is committed to ensuring its people are prepared and prioritized in this transition.

How are we doing that? With real action—not empty promises. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above a $4,000 credit top-up plus a training allowance of up to $3,000 a month for full-time study—a serious boost for mid-career training. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: he went from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer by using SkillsFuture credits and further studies at SUSS—a clear example of how these programmes work on the ground. Meanwhile, Workforce Singapore (WSG) and its partners placed more than 56,000 locals into jobs last fiscal year through expanded job matching services and career fairs tailored for Singaporeans navigating this changing landscape. The government is also working closely with unions—to redesign jobs so workers get equipped with the right skills for tomorrow’s economy. These efforts put Singaporeans front and center in our nation’s AI-driven future.

Looking ahead—Singapore isn’t just adapting; we’re setting ambitious goals under our National AI Strategy 2.0 aiming for AI to contribute about US$29.6 billion to our economy by 2030—that’s no small change at all. A Salesforce study ranked us second globally—and first in Asia-Pacific—for AI readiness, putting us ahead of many bigger economies out there. And in 2025 alone, more than 105,000 people took up SkillsFuture-supported AI-related training courses—a clear sign we’re building a workforce ready for this new era.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to keep our edge globally—it’s essential. But beyond technology or dollars lies what truly makes us strong: our ideas, our innovation—and most importantly—our unity as one nation moving forward together.

Thank you.
