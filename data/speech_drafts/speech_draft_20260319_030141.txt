Fellow Singaporeans,

Not long ago, artificial intelligence — or AI — was something we only read about in science fiction or heard as distant news from overseas. Today, it’s part of our everyday lives here in Singapore. Whether you’re planning a trip and getting personalised travel recommendations from AI, or calling a government agency and having your conversation transcribed and summarised in any of our four official languages, AI is already working quietly behind the scenes. This isn’t some far-off future. It’s happening now. And as we gather this National Day Rally to reflect on our progress and chart our path forward, it’s clear that AI matters to every Singaporean today and will matter even more tomorrow.

Artificial intelligence is shaping up to be one of the defining technologies of our era. Back in 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems — it wasn’t reliable. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete at the International Math Olympiad, performing at a gold medal level. That leap shows just how fast this technology is advancing. Here in Singapore, AI isn’t just an abstract concept; it’s transforming public services and daily life. Our civil service uses AI in agency call centres to transcribe conversations across all four official languages and generate summaries so officers can focus on what really matters: serving you better.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

To do that, we must deploy AI across the economy — not just in big companies but also among small and medium enterprises. Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise to attract high-quality investments and create good jobs for our people.

Take Tuas Port, for example. There, AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, studies are underway to explore automation possibilities using AI. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility right here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components and alert human operators for precise diagnosis—helping improve efficiency without replacing skilled workers.

Even SMEs like Q&M Dental are tapping into AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, or decay faster than before.

These examples show that AI adoption isn’t limited to large corporations; smaller businesses can benefit too.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

It’s a valid concern. History shows us that technological change can disrupt work but also opens doors to new opportunities and better jobs.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy fundamentally. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we embraced it wholeheartedly, propelling ourselves forward.

At PSA terminals today, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

So yes, technology changes jobs—and yes, it can be disruptive—but it also enables workers to move into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach remains people-centred. The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs where needed and equip workers with new skills for emerging roles.

We’re expanding efforts to help Singaporeans access good jobs — through enhanced job matching services, more career fairs at tertiary institutions, and a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will also grow. It provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 worth of SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study over two years.

Let me share one example: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was a private hire driver until he turned 41 in 2025. He used SkillsFuture credits to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS—before transitioning into a Business Intelligence Developer role.

These efforts are about more than individual careers—they’re about building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

Ultimately, a dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all of us.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by chasing trends blindly but by focusing on ideas, innovation, and unity among us all.

We must stay ahead through smart policies grounded in reality—not hype—and by ensuring no one gets left behind as technology changes how we live and work.

This is our challenge—and our opportunity—as we look ahead together.

Thank you.
