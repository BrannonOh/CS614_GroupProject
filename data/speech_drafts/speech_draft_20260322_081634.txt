Good morning, everyone.

Let’s start with something that might surprise you. Just a few years ago, artificial intelligence was a niche tool — something you heard about in tech circles but didn’t see much of in daily life. Today, AI has become a powerful force woven into the fabric of everyday life here in Singapore.

Take Google DeepMind’s AlphaGeometry, for example. This AI system can solve complex geometry problems at the level of the International Mathematical Olympiad — that’s some serious brainpower. And it’s not just academic. Among Singaporean travelers using Generative AI for trip planning, 92% say it saves them time and makes itinerary creation a breeze. Even our civil service is on board: AI helps call centres transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries to assist officers. 

Singaporeans are already living with AI — whether booking a trip or interacting with government services. This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and innovation across Singapore’s economy.

Now, let’s talk about what that means on the ground.

AI isn’t just for big companies or flashy tech startups. It’s transforming industries across the board — from ports to healthcare — and benefiting both large enterprises and small businesses alike.

At Tuas Port, AI combined with robotics and autonomous machines is already in use, while Changi Airport is studying automation possibilities. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to spot anomalies and help human operators diagnose issues more quickly and accurately. Even smaller players like Q&M Dental are tapping into AI to analyze dental X-rays for better detection of dental problems.

Singapore’s unique mix of cutting-edge technology and skilled human expertise doesn’t just attract investments; it creates quality jobs across sectors. The government drives this adoption actively through national strategies and programs designed to support all businesses — including SMEs — making sure no one gets left behind.

But as promising as this sounds, we can’t ignore the elephant in the room: job displacement concerns.

History tells us that technological change shakes things up — it disrupts jobs, creates uncertainty, even anxiety among workers forced to adapt. But it also opens doors to new, higher-skilled roles that didn’t exist before.

Back in 1981, Singapore launched the National Computerisation Plan to computerise the civil service, train software professionals, and build a local IT industry from scratch. Fast forward to the late 1990s: internet adoption exploded from under 14% in 1997 to 36% by 2000. At PSA, crane operators were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes simultaneously — boosting productivity while improving job quality.

These shifts weren’t easy; they caused real disruption and fear. But they also paved the way for better opportunities and new industries we couldn’t have imagined before.

Building on this history, Singapore is putting its people first as we move into the AI era — with targeted support and training designed to help workers thrive amid change.

For instance, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above up to $4,000 in credits plus a training allowance for full-time study — equal to half their average income capped at $3,000 per month. NTUC’s Company Training Committees co-fund up to 70% of companies’ transformation projects; interestingly, AI-related projects more than doubled in 2025 compared to 2024.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer by leveraging SkillsFuture credits and further studies at SUSS.

The government works closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills. Expanded job matching services and career fairs make sure Singaporeans can access fresh opportunities quickly.

All these efforts ensure that Singaporeans remain at the heart of progress as our nation embraces AI for a dynamic future.

Looking ahead, there’s no doubt: AI will reshape our economy profoundly.

A recent Google-commissioned report estimates that AI-powered solutions could add S$198.3 billion — that’s US$146 billion — to Singapore's economy by 2030. Globally, Singapore ranks second on the AI Readiness Index thanks to strong regulatory frameworks, widespread adoption of AI technologies, and an attractive investment environment.

Prime Minister Lawrence Wong has been clear about what matters most: this is a “People-First AI Vision.” Success won’t come just from data or computing power but from our people — their ideas, innovation, and unity.

We’re building a dynamic economy that creates good jobs and improves lives for every Singaporean. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay competitive on the world stage; it’s essential.

So let’s face it head-on: artificial intelligence is here to stay. Our future depends on how well we harness it together — thoughtfully, inclusively, with everyone moving forward side by side.

Thank you.
