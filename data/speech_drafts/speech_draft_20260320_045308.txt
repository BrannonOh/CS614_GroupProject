Good evening, everyone.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—or AI—felt like something out of a sci-fi movie. A distant idea, something for the future. But here in Singapore today, AI is already part of our daily lives. Think about how many of us use AI when planning a trip—getting personalised recommendations on where to go and what to do. Or consider our civil service, where AI listens in on calls in all four official languages, transcribing and summarising so officers can focus on helping you better. This is not some far-off technology; it’s here, now. And as we gather this National Day Rally, it’s clear why this matters to all of us—not just today but for the future we want to build.

Artificial intelligence will be one of the defining technologies of our era. For Singapore, it’s not just about keeping up—it’s about harnessing AI to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean.

Let me put this into perspective. In 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems—it wasn’t reliable. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete at the International Math Olympiad level, even winning gold medals. That’s a leap in just five years. Here at home, AI is already woven into how we live and work—from travel planning apps to government services that help us more efficiently.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

Singapore’s strength has always been its ability to combine technology with human expertise—and that’s why investors bring high-quality projects here, creating good jobs for our people. But let me be clear: adopting AI isn’t just for big companies with deep pockets. Small and medium enterprises—the backbone of our economy—can benefit too.

Look at Tuas Port, where AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, automation is being studied closely to improve efficiency. GE Vernova has a turbine repair facility here that uses AI to detect tiny anomalies in turbine parts—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses. And SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings or tooth decay that might be missed otherwise.

This shows how AI can add value across industries big and small.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

This concern is not new. Technological change has always disrupted work—but it has also created new opportunities and better jobs. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it quickly and propelled ourselves forward.

At PSA ports today, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

History tells us that while technology changes the nature of work, it doesn’t eliminate opportunity—it shifts it toward higher-skilled roles with better pay.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will remain people-centred. The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills suited for an AI-driven economy.

We’re expanding job matching services and holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions. We’re also launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils—to bring opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowances and courses from industry leaders and private providers.

To give you an example: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was a private hire driver until recently. At age 41 in 2025, he used his SkillsFuture credits to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. Today, he works as a Business Intelligence Developer—a career upgrade made possible by these programmes.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to two years.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all of us—not just a few at the top.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by chasing trends blindly but by leading through ideas and innovation grounded in real skills.

We want Singapore not only to keep pace but to stay ahead—through unity as much as ingenuity.

So as we look forward from this National Day Rally moment—let’s face it straight: artificial intelligence isn’t coming tomorrow; it’s here today. The choice is ours—to harness it wisely or fall behind.

Together, let’s make sure Singapore harnesses AI not just as technology but as a tool for progress that benefits every single one of us.

Thank you.
