Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was a distant idea—something we only saw in science fiction or the news. Today, AI is no longer a far-off concept. It’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. We use it when we plan our holidays, getting personalised recommendations for destinations and customised itineraries. In our public service, AI is hard at work too. Our civil service call centres transcribe conversations in all four official languages—English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil—and generate summaries so officers can spend more time helping you directly.

This rapid progress is striking. Back in 2020, AI struggled even with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete in the International Math Olympiad—performing at a gold medal level. That’s how fast this technology is moving.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. This National Day Rally moment calls on all of us to reflect on why this matters—not just now but for our future as a nation. The real question for Singapore is how we harness AI to raise productivity, strengthen competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean.

Artificial intelligence will be a defining technology of our era. And Singapore must use it to raise productivity, boost competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

We’ve already seen how combining technology with human expertise attracts high-quality investments that create good jobs here in Singapore. AI isn’t just for big corporations; small and medium enterprises stand to gain too.

Take Tuas Port, where AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, AI is being studied carefully for automation to improve efficiency without disrupting service quality. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components and alert human experts for precise diagnosis—saving time and improving quality.

Even SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI tools to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay better than before.

These examples show the real value AI brings across sectors and company sizes by raising productivity and creating new value.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

It’s a fair concern—and one we must face head-on.

Technological change has always caused disruption. Workers worry about job losses and skills becoming obsolete. But history shows us something else: these disruptions also opened doors for workers to move into higher-skilled and better-paying jobs.

In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—a political decision that transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it decisively and built new industries along with it.

At PSA, crane operators who once worked on-site were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once. This boosted productivity while improving job quality—not by cutting jobs but by upgrading them.

Yes, technological shifts are tough—but they don’t have to mean fewer jobs or lower living standards. Instead, they can open doors to better roles that pay more.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations—from computers to the internet—we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will remain people-centred.

The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs so workers are equipped with relevant skills for the new roles created by AI adoption.

We’re expanding job matching services, holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions, and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils—to help Singaporeans find good jobs close to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowances and courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

This programme already provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example—a 41-year-old in 2025 who transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to get a diploma in data engineering—and then continuing his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

This isn’t just about training or job placement—it’s about putting people first as we adopt new technology.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic—one that creates good jobs and better lives for every Singaporean.

Embracing AI supports this goal by helping Singapore stay globally competitive—not by chance but by design—with ideas that lead innovation—and unity that strengthens us all.

To those who say technology will leave workers behind or hollow out our economy—I say this: history does not repeat itself if we refuse it permission. We have faced disruption before—and emerged stronger because we planned ahead and put people first.

This National Day Rally moment calls on us not just to reflect on how far we’ve come—but also on how we move forward together into this new era shaped by artificial intelligence.

Thank you.
