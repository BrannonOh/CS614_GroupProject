Have you ever wondered how Singapore manages to keep its lights on, even when global conflicts erupt far from our shores? This question is more than rhetorical—it points to a critical challenge for our nation. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas. This heavy reliance on imported energy, particularly natural gas, exposes us to vulnerabilities. When conflicts arise in key regions like the Middle East, supply chains can be disrupted, causing fluctuations in availability and price. Such disruptions have direct consequences for our economy and daily lives. Having established these risks, let’s explore how Singapore stays resilient despite these challenges.

To mitigate these vulnerabilities, Singapore has adopted a multifaceted strategy focused on diversification and infrastructure strength. Our natural gas imports come from multiple countries—including Indonesia and Malaysia—delivered through pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. Beyond piped gas, we secure supplies from major LNG exporters such as Australia, Qatar, and the United States. This diversification reduces dependency on any single source or region. Additionally, Singapore has invested in robust infrastructure: our LNG terminal boasts a peak send-out capacity of around 11 million tonnes per annum, and we maintain strategic energy stockpiles sufficient to cover several months of demand. These measures provide buffers against short-term shocks and supply interruptions. With these strategies in place, Singapore is also looking ahead to secure its energy future sustainably.

Looking forward, Singapore is actively pursuing renewable and low-carbon initiatives to enhance energy security while addressing environmental imperatives. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30% of our projected demand—from regional sources. This includes plans to import 1 gigawatt of hydropower from Sarawak in Malaysia and solar energy from interconnected grids in the region. Concurrently, efforts are underway to develop low-carbon alternatives such as hydrogen; for example, Keppel is constructing a 600-megawatt hydrogen-ready power plant aligned with Singapore’s National Hydrogen Strategy. Improving energy efficiency remains a priority as well. These concrete projects represent strategic steps toward a more resilient and sustainable energy system. But energy security is not just about infrastructure and technology; it also depends on each of us.

Energy security extends beyond government policy—it requires active participation from households and businesses alike. In Singapore, households account for about 15% of total electricity consumption. Therefore, conservation efforts at home can meaningfully contribute to national resilience. Building awareness around responsible energy use strengthens our collective ability to withstand external shocks. National campaigns such as the Go25 movement encourage citizens to set air-conditioners at 25 degrees Celsius as a practical step toward reducing consumption without compromising comfort. These initiatives exemplify how individual actions complement broader strategies in maintaining energy security. Finally, let’s consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and cost.

Singapore faces the complex task of balancing three interrelated priorities: reliable energy supply, environmental sustainability, and affordability—often referred to as the energy trilemma. Our policies must carefully navigate these sometimes competing objectives to ensure long-term stability and progress. Since 2019, Singapore has implemented a carbon tax that provides an economic signal encouraging businesses and consumers to reduce carbon-intensive activities while supporting sustainable growth. Long-term planning remains essential so that even amid global uncertainties, Singapore can maintain secure access to affordable and clean energy resources. In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security is a shared journey requiring strategy, innovation, and collective effort—one that depends on prudent policy decisions as well as the commitment of every individual in this room and across the nation.
