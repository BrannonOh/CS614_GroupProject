Today, I want to begin by addressing a concern that many Singaporeans share: our energy security in an increasingly uncertain world. We rely heavily on imported energy—over 95% of our electricity needs come from natural gas brought in from abroad. This dependence makes us vulnerable to disruptions beyond our control. Conflicts in critical regions like the Middle East can unsettle global energy supplies, causing fluctuations in availability and prices that ripple through our economy and daily lives. These are not distant issues; they have direct implications for Singapore’s stability and prosperity.

Having understood the risks, let's explore how Singapore stays resilient despite these challenges.

Singapore’s approach to energy security is grounded in strategic diversification and robust infrastructure. We import natural gas from multiple countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, through both pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. This multi-source strategy reduces reliance on any single supplier or region. Moreover, our LNG terminal has a peak send-out capacity of around 11 million tonnes per annum, which provides a significant buffer against short-term shocks in supply. Together with strategic reserves, these measures form a solid foundation that helps us withstand fluctuations in the global market.

Building on this resilience, Singapore is also planning for a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Looking ahead, Singapore is actively investing in low-carbon energy alternatives to enhance our long-term energy security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30% of our projected demand. A key initiative is the Lao PDR-Thailand-Malaysia-Singapore Power Integration Project, or LTMS-PIP, which represents ASEAN’s first multilateral cross-border electricity trade. This project facilitates renewable energy imports such as solar power from regional grids. Alongside this, we are developing low-carbon alternatives like hydrogen and advancing improvements in energy efficiency across sectors to reduce overall demand.

While technology and policy are vital, the role of individuals and society is equally important.

Energy security is not solely the responsibility of policymakers or industry; it requires active participation from households and businesses alike. In 2023, households consumed about 8 terawatt hours of electricity—roughly 14.5% of Singapore’s total consumption. This means that responsible energy use at home can have a tangible impact on national resilience. By raising awareness and encouraging conservation practices, we collectively strengthen our ability to manage demand during periods of supply stress.

Finally, let's consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and cost concerns.

Our energy policy must carefully balance three key objectives: reliable supply, environmental sustainability, and affordability for consumers and businesses. An example of this balance is our carbon tax policy, which will increase to S$45 per ton by 2026. This measure incentivizes emissions reductions while considering economic impacts on companies and households. Long-term planning remains essential as we navigate global uncertainties—from geopolitical tensions to technological shifts—to secure Singapore’s energy future without compromising growth or environmental commitments.

In conclusion, Singapore’s comprehensive approach—rooted in diversification, innovation, infrastructure resilience, forward-looking investments, public participation, and balanced policy—ensures that despite global disruptions, our energy security remains strong. It is through this multifaceted strategy that we can confidently face the challenges ahead together.

Thank you.
