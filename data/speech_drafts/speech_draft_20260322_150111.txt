Have you ever wondered how secure Singapore’s energy supply really is, especially in times of global conflict? It’s a pressing question, given how much we rely on external sources to power our daily lives. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas, making us heavily dependent on foreign energy supplies. Recent conflicts in the Middle East have caused global liquefied natural gas (LNG) prices to rise sharply, directly impacting the cost of electricity here at home. This dependence exposes us to vulnerabilities that could affect both our economy and households. Having established this vulnerability, let’s explore how Singapore remains resilient despite these challenges.

Singapore’s approach to energy security is grounded in strategic diversification and robust infrastructure. We import natural gas from multiple countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, using both pipelines and LNG shipments. Beyond piped gas, our LNG imports are diversified as well, with Australia being a major supplier among others. This multi-source strategy reduces our reliance on any single region and spreads risk effectively. Furthermore, we are strengthening our infrastructure—plans for a second LNG terminal are underway to provide additional capacity and buffer against short-term supply shocks. These measures ensure that even when disruptions occur elsewhere, Singapore’s energy supply remains stable. Looking ahead, Singapore is also transitioning towards a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Central to this future is our commitment to importing low-carbon electricity and developing alternative energy sources. By 2035, Singapore aims to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30% of our projected demand—through large-scale renewable projects such as the Australia-Asia PowerLink and agreements with countries like Indonesia and Vietnam. Alongside these imports, we have launched a National Hydrogen Strategy and are investing significantly in research and development to establish hydrogen as a key component of our future energy mix. These efforts complement ongoing initiatives to enhance energy efficiency across sectors. Together, they position Singapore not only to meet rising demand but also to reduce carbon emissions responsibly. But energy security is not just about infrastructure and technology; it also depends on the actions of individuals and society.

In fact, public participation plays a critical role in strengthening our national resilience. In 2023, households accounted for approximately 14.5% of Singapore’s total electricity consumption—a substantial share that highlights the impact of everyday energy use. Energy security is therefore a shared responsibility involving both households and businesses through conscious conservation efforts. National campaigns such as Go Green SG promote awareness of responsible energy usage, encouraging all of us to contribute toward reducing demand peaks and overall consumption. Such collective action complements government strategies by easing pressure on supply systems during critical periods. Finally, we must consider how Singapore balances security, sustainability, and cost in its energy policies.

This balance—often referred to as the “energy trilemma”—is among the most complex challenges we face. Singapore must ensure reliable energy supply while advancing environmental goals and maintaining affordability for consumers. Our carbon tax policy exemplifies this tension: increases designed to promote sustainability are expected to raise household utility bills in the near term. Navigating these competing priorities requires careful long-term planning amid global uncertainty about market conditions and geopolitical developments. Only through sustained commitment can we maintain security without compromising economic competitiveness or environmental responsibilities. In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security is a dynamic achievement requiring strategy, innovation, and collective effort.

To summarize: Singapore ensures its energy security through diversification of supply sources, investment in innovative low-carbon technologies, robust infrastructure development, and active public participation in conservation efforts. Every individual has a role in sustaining this security for the nation’s future—a shared responsibility that calls on all of us to remain informed and engaged as we navigate an evolving global landscape together.
