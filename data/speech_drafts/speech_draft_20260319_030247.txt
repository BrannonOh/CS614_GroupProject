Fellow Singaporeans,

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—felt like something from science fiction. Today, it’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. Many of you have used AI to plan trips, where it suggests destinations and even creates personalised itineraries just for you. When you call government agencies, AI is at work too—transcribing conversations in all four official languages and summarising them so officers can spend more time helping you directly. As we gather at this National Day Rally—a time to reflect on how far we’ve come and where we’re headed—it’s clear: AI is no longer a distant idea. It matters to every Singaporean now, and it will matter even more in the years ahead.

Artificial intelligence is shaping up to be one of the defining technologies of our era. Think about this: In 2020, AI struggled to solve basic math problems reliably. By 2025, it could compete in the International Math Olympiad—and perform at a gold medal level. That’s a leap that shows how fast AI is advancing. Here in Singapore, AI isn’t just theory; it’s transforming public services and daily life. Our civil service uses AI in agency call centres to transcribe conversations across all four official languages and generate summaries—helping officers focus on serving you better.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

To do that, we need to deploy AI across the whole economy—not just big companies but also small and medium enterprises. Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise. This mix attracts quality investments and creates meaningful jobs for our people.

Look at Tuas Port. There, AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines to keep operations running smoothly. At Changi Airport, AI is being tested for automation—exploring ways to improve efficiency without compromising safety or service quality. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts and alert human operators for precise checks—helping workers do their jobs better.

Even smaller businesses like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—detecting fillings, impacted teeth, or decay faster than before.

These examples show that adopting AI isn’t just for big corporations; SMEs benefit too. And by combining technology with human skills, Singapore attracts investments that create solid jobs across sectors.

But whenever new technology changes industries, people ask a crucial question: what will this mean for jobs?

It’s a fair concern. History tells us that technological change disrupts work but also opens new doors—to better jobs requiring higher skills.

In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that gave students and workers computer skills—and transformed our economy. When the internet came along in the 1990s, we embraced it fully and moved forward quickly.

Today at PSA terminals, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—raising productivity while improving job quality by giving them more control and oversight.

So yes, technology changes jobs—and yes, it can disrupt—but it also helps workers move into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

And just as we supported workers through past shifts, we must do the same now with artificial intelligence.

Our approach stays people-centred. The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs where needed—and equip workers with new skills for emerging roles.

We’re stepping up efforts to help Singaporeans find good jobs: expanding job matching services, holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions, and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too. It gives Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 worth of SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study—for as long as 24 months—and soon will include more part-time training allowance claims along with courses from industry leaders and private providers.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example. At 41 in 2025, he shifted from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and then continuing his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

These efforts are about more than individual careers—they’re about building an economy that stays dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs and better lives for all of us.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by chasing trends blindly but by focusing on ideas, innovation—and unity among us all.

We must stay ahead through smart policies grounded in reality—not hype—and by making sure no one gets left behind as technology changes how we live and work.

This is both our challenge—and our chance—as we look ahead together.

Thank you.
