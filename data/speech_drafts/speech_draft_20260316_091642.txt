Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—was a distant idea. Something you read about in science fiction or heard about in far-off labs. Today, it’s everywhere. AI is no longer a concept; it’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. Think about how many of us use AI just to plan a holiday—AI recommends destinations and creates personalized itineraries tailored to our tastes. In our public services, AI is hard at work too. In agency call centres across the civil service, AI transcribes conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries so officers can spend more time actually helping you. And here’s something striking: back in 2020, AI struggled even with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete—and perform at gold medal level—in the International Math Olympiad. That’s not just progress; that’s a leap forward.

This shows how fast AI is advancing and why it matters to every Singaporean today and for the future. Especially now, as we gather at this National Day Rally—a moment to reflect on where we stand and where we’re headed.

Artificial intelligence will be one of the defining technologies of our era. And Singapore must harness it—to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is this: how do we harness AI to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world?

Singapore has always been strong at combining technology with human expertise—that mix attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs for our people. Look at Tuas Port: AI works hand-in-hand with robotics and autonomous machines to speed up operations and improve efficiency. At Changi Airport, we’re studying how automation powered by AI can help us run smoother and smarter. Over in industry, GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components early—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses and repairs. It’s not just the big players either; small and medium enterprises benefit too. Take Q&M Dental—they use AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay that might otherwise be missed.

This shows something important: adopting AI isn’t just for giant companies; SMEs can tap into its power too.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, there’s a question on everyone’s mind: what will this mean for jobs?

We’ve faced this before. Yes, technological change disrupts lives sometimes—but history also tells us this: it creates new opportunities and better jobs.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—a bold move that transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate; we adopted it quickly and used it as a springboard for growth.

Look at PSA today: crane operators who once worked directly on-site are now retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously. This shift doesn’t just boost productivity—it improves job quality by reducing physical strain while increasing responsibility and oversight.

Technological change has always been disruptive—but it also lifts workers into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again now—in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will be people-centred from start to finish.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs around AI adoption—and equip workers with new skills they need to stay relevant. We’re expanding job matching services—more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils (CDCs) to connect Singaporeans with opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowance claims alongside an expanded range of courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

Here’s an example: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was 41 years old in 2025 when he made a career switch—from private hire driver to Business Intelligence Developer. He used SkillsFuture credits first for a diploma in data engineering, then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS—all while working towards better prospects.

Remember—the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

These aren’t just programs—they’re concrete support designed specifically around the challenges posed by new technologies like AI.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy means good jobs—and better lives—for all of us—not just some of us.

Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay globally competitive; it’s essential. But more than staying competitive—it’s about staying ahead through ideas, innovation—and unity among all Singaporeans.

Some say AI will take away jobs or leave people behind. I say: if we don’t face this head-on—if we don’t shape how AI fits into our economy—we risk falling behind instead.

AI is not just technology; it’s part of our future together. And together is how we’ll succeed.

Thank you.
