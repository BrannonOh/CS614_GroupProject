Have you ever wondered how Singapore manages to keep its lights on, even when global conflicts erupt far from our shores? This is not merely a rhetorical question; it highlights a critical challenge we face as a nation. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas, underscoring our heavy reliance on imported energy. Such dependence inevitably exposes us to vulnerabilities. When conflicts occur in key regions, like the Middle East, supply chains can be disrupted, leading not only to interruptions in availability but also to significant fluctuations in prices. These disruptions have direct and tangible impacts on our economy and daily lives. Having established these risks, let us now explore how Singapore stays resilient despite these challenges.

To mitigate these vulnerabilities, Singapore has adopted a multifaceted strategy centered on diversification and infrastructure robustness. Our natural gas imports come from multiple countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, delivered through both pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. Beyond piped gas, we secure supplies from major LNG exporters such as Australia, Qatar, and the United States. This deliberate diversification reduces our dependency on any single source or region, thereby enhancing supply security. In addition to diversifying sources, Singapore has invested in strong infrastructure: our LNG terminal boasts a peak send-out capacity of approximately 11 million tonnes per annum. Complementing this capacity are strategic energy stockpiles maintained to cover several months of demand. These measures collectively provide essential buffers against short-term shocks and potential supply interruptions. With these strategies firmly in place, Singapore is also looking ahead to secure its energy future sustainably.

Looking forward, Singapore is actively pursuing renewable and low-carbon initiatives to enhance energy security while addressing environmental imperatives. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—about 30% of our projected demand—from regional sources. This includes concrete plans such as importing 1 gigawatt of hydropower from Sarawak in Malaysia and solar energy from interconnected grids within the region. Concurrently, efforts are underway to develop low-carbon alternatives like hydrogen; for instance, Keppel is constructing a 600-megawatt hydrogen-ready power plant aligned with Singapore’s National Hydrogen Strategy. Improving energy efficiency remains a core priority alongside these initiatives. These tangible projects represent strategic steps toward building a more resilient and sustainable energy system for the nation. But it is important to recognize that energy security is not solely about infrastructure and technology; it also depends on each one of us.

Energy security extends beyond government policy—it requires active participation from households and businesses alike. In Singapore, households account for about 15% of total electricity consumption, which means that conservation efforts at home can meaningfully contribute to national resilience. Building awareness around responsible energy use strengthens our collective ability to withstand external shocks. National campaigns such as the Go25 movement encourage citizens to set air-conditioners at 25 degrees Celsius—a practical step toward reducing consumption without compromising comfort. These initiatives exemplify how individual actions complement broader strategies in maintaining our energy security. Finally, let us consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and cost.

Singapore faces the complex task of balancing three interrelated priorities: reliable energy supply, environmental sustainability, and affordability—often referred to as the energy trilemma. Our policies must carefully navigate these sometimes competing objectives to ensure long-term stability and progress. Since 2019, Singapore has implemented a carbon tax that provides an economic signal encouraging businesses and consumers to reduce carbon-intensive activities while supporting sustainable growth. Long-term planning remains essential so that even amid global uncertainties, Singapore can maintain secure access to affordable and clean energy resources. In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security is a shared journey requiring strategy grounded in diversification and innovation—as well as collective effort by all sectors of society—to ensure that we remain resilient now and into the future.
