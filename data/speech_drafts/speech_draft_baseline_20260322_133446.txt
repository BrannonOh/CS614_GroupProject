Ladies and gentlemen,

It’s remarkable to reflect on how far artificial intelligence has come in such a short time. Not long ago, in 2020, AI struggled even with basic math problems, unable to provide reliable solutions. Fast forward to 2025, and AI systems are now competing at the highest levels, achieving gold medal-level performance in the International Math Olympiad. This rapid progress isn’t just theoretical—it’s happening right here in Singapore and impacting our daily lives. Many of us already rely on AI for practical tasks like planning our travels, where it can recommend destinations tailored just for us and generate personalised itineraries that make our trips smoother and more enjoyable. Beyond personal use, government agencies have embraced AI to enhance public service efficiency. For example, call centres within Singapore’s civil service use AI to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and produce summaries, freeing officers to focus more on helping the public. This rapid advancement and integration of AI opens new possibilities for boosting productivity and creating value across Singapore's economy.

Singapore stands at the forefront of combining cutting-edge technology with human expertise to attract quality investments and create good jobs for its people. Across various sectors, AI is driving productivity gains that benefit not only large corporations but also small and medium enterprises. Take Tuas Port, where AI is integrated with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations, or Changi Airport, where automation technologies are being actively studied to improve efficiency. In the energy sector, GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components early on, alerting human operators who then perform precise diagnostics—demonstrating a powerful collaboration between humans and machines. Even smaller businesses are harnessing AI’s potential: Q&M Dental employs AI tools to analyze dental X-rays more accurately, helping dentists detect fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay with greater precision. This shows that adopting AI is not limited to big players; SMEs can also access these technologies to improve their services and competitiveness. While AI brings these benefits, it also raises concerns about job displacement, which we must address thoughtfully.

It’s natural to worry about how new technology might affect jobs. But history teaches us that while technological change can disrupt work initially, it also creates opportunities for workers to move into higher-skilled and better-paying roles. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with essential computer skills—a move that transformed our economy over time. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, Singapore embraced it wholeheartedly, propelling the nation forward into a new digital age. More recently at PSA Corporation, crane operators were retrained to remotely supervise and manage multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—a shift that not only boosted productivity but also improved job quality by giving workers more oversight and control over complex operations. Building on this legacy of adaptation and growth, the government is now prioritising support for Singaporeans so they can thrive in the emerging AI era through targeted initiatives.

To ensure inclusive growth in this new era, the government is working closely with unions to redesign jobs and provide fresh skills training tailored for the future economy. Expanded job matching services and career fairs are helping Singaporeans access new opportunities aligned with evolving industry needs. One standout initiative is the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme, which offers substantial financial support and training pathways specifically designed for mid-career workers looking to upskill or reskill. For example, under this programme—available to Singaporeans aged 40 and above—individuals receive $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits as well as training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months. Consider Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: at 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer after using his SkillsFuture credits to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and he continues his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS today. Stories like Vinoth’s demonstrate how these efforts are paving the way for a dynamic and competitive Singapore that embraces AI for the benefit of all.

Looking ahead, we envision a future-ready Singapore where innovation fuels a dynamic economy that creates good jobs and improves lives for every citizen. Embracing artificial intelligence thoughtfully and inclusively is essential—not just as a tool for growth but as a foundation for securing our nation’s global competitiveness in an ever-changing world. Ultimately, Singapore’s future success will depend on ideas sparked by innovation—and on our unity as a people working together toward shared progress.

Thank you.
