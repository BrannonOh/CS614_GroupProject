Good morning, everyone.

Let’s start with something striking. Just a few years ago, in 2020, AI couldn’t reliably solve basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and AI systems are competing in the International Math Olympiad — and not just competing, but performing at a gold medal level. That’s a leap you don’t see every day. Right here in Singapore, AI isn’t some distant future technology; it’s already woven into our daily lives. Our civil service uses AI in agency call centres to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generate summaries that help officers focus on what really matters—serving the public better. And it’s not just government work. According to a recent study by Booking.com, over half of Singaporean travellers—55%—are keen to use AI tools for planning their trips and making travel smoother. In just a few years, AI has transformed from a struggling technology into something deeply integrated into how we live and work here.

This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and create new value across Singapore's economy.

Now, let’s talk about what this means for our industries. AI is not just about flashy tech; it’s about real impact on the ground. At Tuas Port, they combine AI with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. Over at Changi Airport, they’re studying automation to improve efficiency. Even GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts and alert human operators early—helping them diagnose problems with precision before they become costly failures. And it’s not only big players benefiting from this tech leap. SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—to detect fillings, impacted teeth, tooth decay—you name it. This mix of technology and human expertise is attracting high-quality investments and creating good jobs across the board—from large firms down to small businesses.

But while AI drives growth and innovation, we can’t ignore the elephant in the room: job displacement. It’s a real concern that we have to face head-on.

Here’s the thing: technological change has always disrupted jobs—but it also creates new opportunities. Look back to the 1980s when Singapore launched its nationwide computerisation drive. Students and workers picked up new computer skills that transformed our economy. At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—a huge productivity boost that also made their work more skilled and rewarding. Manufacturing tells a similar story: with 730 industrial robots per 10,000 workers—a very high automation rate—we’ve seen production output jump by 20%. These aren’t just numbers; they’re proof that while tech changes how we work, it doesn’t have to mean fewer or worse jobs.

Building on this history, Singapore is prioritising support for workers to thrive in the AI transition.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills for this changing landscape. Take the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme: it offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in credits plus up to $3,000 per month in training allowances for full-time study—real support for mid-career learning. Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is one example—at 41 years old in 2025, he moved from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer by using SkillsFuture credits and further studies at SUSS. That kind of career shift shows what’s possible when you get the right support. There are other efforts too: more career fairs at tertiary institutions; expanded job matching services; and a brand-new initiative called 'Jobs Nearby @ CDC' that helps unemployed residents find jobs closer to home within their communities. Plus, partners like 37 ‘SkillsFuture Queen Bee’ companies are providing curated training programs reaching over 5,200 enterprises nationwide.

With these efforts underway, Singapore is poised to embrace AI for a dynamic and competitive future.

Looking ahead, adopting AI isn’t just about staying current—it’s about securing our future prosperity. Experts project that by 2030, AI adoption could bring up to SGD 198.3 billion in economic benefits right here in Singapore—that’s a game changer for our economy. Prime Minister Lawrence Wong put it plainly: “We will exploit AI to grow the economy, and we will ensure that growth translates into good jobs and better wages.” That sums up our challenge—and our opportunity—to build an economy that works for everyone.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans. Embracing AI is essential if we want to remain globally competitive—not just today but decades from now. And Singapore aims not only to keep pace but stay ahead through ideas, innovation—and above all—unity.

Thank you.
