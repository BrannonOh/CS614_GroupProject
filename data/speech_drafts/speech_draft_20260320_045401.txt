Good evening, everyone.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—felt like something far off in the future. Today in Singapore, it’s part of our everyday lives. When you plan a trip, AI can recommend destinations and generate personalised itineraries tailored just for you. In our civil service, AI transcribes calls in all four official languages and creates summaries so officers can spend more time helping you directly. As we come together at this National Day Rally—a moment to reflect on our progress and chart the way forward—it’s clear that AI matters to every Singaporean now and for the future we want to build.

Artificial intelligence will be a defining technology of our era. For Singapore, keeping pace isn’t enough. We must harness AI to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

Think about this: in 2020, AI struggled to solve basic math problems reliably. By 2025, it can compete in the International Math Olympiad—and perform at a gold medal level. That’s rapid progress. Right here at home, AI is already woven into how we live and work—from travel planning apps that recommend where to go, to government call centres that transcribe and summarise conversations across languages.

So AI is no longer distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise. This unique mix attracts high-quality investments—and creates good jobs for our people. And let me be clear: AI adoption isn’t just for large companies with deep pockets; small and medium enterprises—our economy’s backbone—can benefit as well.

Look at Tuas Port: AI is combined with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations. At Changi Airport, AI is being studied specifically for automation to improve efficiency and service quality. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components—alerting human operators who then diagnose issues precisely. And SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—detecting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay that might otherwise be missed.

These examples show how AI raises productivity and creates value across industries big and small.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

This concern is valid—and not new. Technological change has always disrupted work but also enabled workers to move into higher-skilled and better-paying jobs.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—and transformed our economy over that decade. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, Singapore adopted it successfully—and propelled the nation forward.

Today at PSA ports, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

History shows us that technology shifts work toward better jobs; it doesn’t erase opportunity.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do so again now—in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach will remain people-centred. The government will work closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills suited for an AI-driven economy.

We’re expanding job matching services, holding more career fairs at tertiary institutions, and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils to bring opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow too—offering more part-time training allowances and more courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

To give you a concrete example: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was a private hire driver until recently. At age 41 in 2025, he used his SkillsFuture credits to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. Today he works as a Business Intelligence Developer—a clear career upgrade made possible by these programmes.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to 24 months of full-time study.

Ultimately, these efforts are about building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all—not just some or a privileged few.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by blindly chasing trends but by leading through ideas grounded in real skills.

Singapore aims not only to keep pace but stay ahead—through ideas, innovation, and unity.

At this National Day Rally moment—a time for honest reflection—we face a choice: how do we adopt AI so every Singaporean benefits from progress?

If we hesitate or fail to act decisively on AI now, we risk losing ground economically—and with it, opportunities for our people.

But if government agencies collaborate closely with unions; if workers seize chances to upgrade skills; if businesses innovate responsibly—we can shape an economy where technology empowers everyone.

Think of AI as a powerful tool—it can either widen divides or narrow them depending on how we use it.

Our task is clear: make sure it narrows divides—for every worker, every family, every community across Singapore.

Thank you.
