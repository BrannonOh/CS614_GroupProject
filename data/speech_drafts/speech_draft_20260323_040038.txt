Good evening, everyone.

Let’s start with an observation: AI used to struggle with the simplest tasks. Now? It’s everywhere in Singapore—changing how we travel, how government services work, and more. In 2025, AI models from Google and OpenAI didn’t just compete—they scored gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad, a contest designed for expert human reasoning. Meanwhile, a study that same year found that 55% of Singaporean travellers are already turning to AI to plan trips—getting personalised itineraries and activity suggestions tailored just for them. And in government call centres, GovTech’s ‘Transcribe’ platform automatically transcribes conversations in all four official languages plus Singlish. That frees up public officers to focus on what really matters: serving you better. From barely functional tools to powerful helpers integrated into daily life—that’s the AI journey here. This rapid evolution sets the stage for how AI is boosting productivity and creating new value across industries in Singapore.

Now, let’s get real about what this means for our economy and jobs. Take GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here—they use AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts and alert human operators for precise checks. It’s not just the big players getting smarter; SMEs like Q&M Dental analyse dental X-rays with AI to detect fillings, impacted teeth, and decay more accurately than ever before. Then there’s Tuas Port—the world’s largest fully automated container-handling port—where AI and robotics push throughput and efficiency to new levels. Closer to home, Changi Airport is trialling AI-powered security systems that speed up checks by as much as 50%, plus deploying autonomous baggage tractors that boost tarmac productivity. What does this tell us? Singapore’s unique mix of tech and human skill attracts quality investments—and good jobs—to our shores. And it’s not just giants benefiting; SMEs are catching on fast. But let’s not kid ourselves—AI also raises tough questions about job displacement. We have to face those head-on.

History shows us how technological change shakes things up but also opens doors. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—and transformed our economy. Today at PSA, crane operators aren’t just moving cranes—they’ve been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once, boosting productivity while improving job quality. Meanwhile, the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) and the Institute of Banking and Finance (IBF) have teamed up with 11 leading financial institutions—including DBS, OCBC, and UOB—to pilot workforce transformation and upskilling initiatives preparing workers for an AI-enabled future. The message is clear: yes, disruption happens—but it also pushes workers into higher-skilled, better-paying roles. Building on this track record, Singapore is committed to supporting workers through this AI transition with targeted initiatives.

And we’re not just talking—we’re putting real support on the ground. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme gives Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: at 41 in 2025, he switched from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. Beyond individuals like Vinoth, NTUC partners with companies through Company Training Committees (CTCs), providing grants covering up to 70% of costs related to AI adoption—including job redesign and skills training—with unions closely involved in these efforts. On top of training support, job matching services are expanding significantly—with Community Development Councils (CDCs) now leading town-level initiatives that connect job seekers directly with vacancies close to home—making it easier than ever to find work nearby. This tight collaboration between government agencies, unions, employers—and you—is how we make sure no one gets left behind as jobs evolve.

With these strong supports in place, Singapore is ready—not just willing—to embrace AI for a dynamic and competitive future.

Why does this matter? Because according to a 2025 Salesforce study, Singapore ranks second globally in AI readiness—excelling across regulatory readiness, actual AI adoption on the ground, and investment environments that attract serious players. Under our National AI Strategy 2.0, we’re investing over S$1 billion over five years to boost public AI research and development capabilities—strengthening our position as a global leader in this space. A key goal? To more than triple our AI practitioner workforce—to 15,000 strong—and establish Singapore as a global hub for creators shaping tomorrow’s technologies.

Bottom line: a dynamic economy powered by AI creates good jobs—and real opportunities—for Singaporeans who are ready to step up or adapt. We’re not chasing shiny trends blindly; we’re leading pragmatically through ideas, innovation—and yes—unity too.

Thank you.
