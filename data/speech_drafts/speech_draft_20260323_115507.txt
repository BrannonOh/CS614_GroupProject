Let’s start with something striking. Just a few years ago, back in 2020, AI struggled to solve basic math problems reliably. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete in the International Math Olympiad — and not just compete, but perform at a gold medal level. That’s a leap few would have predicted. But it’s not just about math contests. AI is already woven into the daily lives of Singaporeans. Need help planning your next trip? AI can recommend destinations and whip up personalised itineraries tailored just for you. And in our civil service, there’s an AI tool called Transcribe working quietly behind the scenes in agency call centres. It transcribes conversations in all four official languages — and yes, even Singlish — then generates summaries so officers can focus on what really matters: serving the public efficiently.

AI has transformed dramatically in just a few years, becoming part of everyday experience here in Singapore. This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and innovation across Singapore's economy.

Now, let’s talk about how this plays out on the ground. Across industries, AI is not working alone — it teams up with robotics and autonomous machines at places like Tuas Port, while Changi Airport is studying automation possibilities closely. Take GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility right here in Singapore: they use AI to detect anomalies in turbine components and alert human operators for precise diagnosis — catching problems before they become costly failures.

And it’s not just big players benefiting from AI. SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately — spotting fillings, impacted teeth, tooth decay — all helping dentists provide better care.

Singapore is investing heavily to keep this momentum going: over S$1 billion in public AI research between 2025 and 2030. That’s on top of billions more from private investments by giants like Google, Microsoft, and Alibaba Cloud who see Singapore as a hub where technology meets human expertise.

This unique blend attracts quality investments and creates good jobs — not only for large companies but also for smaller enterprises eager to adopt new tools. While AI drives growth, it also raises concerns about job displacement that we must address.

Let’s be clear: technological change always disrupts jobs. But history shows it also opens doors to better opportunities. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills — transforming our economy fundamentally.

At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once — boosting productivity and improving job quality at the same time.

Between 1981 and 1995, this kind of productivity growth accounted for 60% of Singapore's average economic growth of 7.6%. Change isn’t easy — but it can lead to higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

Building on this history, Singapore is prioritising support for workers to thrive in the AI transition.

That means real action today: The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above up to $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to two years.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example. At 41 years old in 2025, he shifted from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and he’s continuing his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

Beyond individual efforts, NTUC’s Company Training Committees bring union leaders together with company management to plan business transformation collaboratively. There are grants available too—supporting AI projects linked directly to job redesign and skills training.

Expanded job matching services and career fairs are also helping people find new opportunities faster than ever before.

The government isn’t leaving anyone behind; it works hand-in-hand with unions and businesses to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills for tomorrow’s economy.

With these efforts underway, Singapore is ready to embrace AI for a future that benefits all citizens.

Looking ahead, the stakes are high—and so are the rewards. According to projections, AI adoption here could bring economic benefits worth up to S$198.3 billion by 2030.

That kind of dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for every Singaporean.

But make no mistake: embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay globally competitive. We must lead through ideas, through innovation—and through unity as one nation ready for what lies ahead.

Thank you.
