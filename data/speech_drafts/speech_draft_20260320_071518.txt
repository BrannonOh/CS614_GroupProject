Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Artificial intelligence used to be a distant idea—something out of sci-fi or tech journals. Today, it’s part of our daily lives here in Singapore. Just think about planning a trip: AI now recommends destinations and even generates personalised itineraries tailored to your preferences. In our public services, AI listens in on calls across all four official languages, transcribing conversations and producing summaries so officers can focus on serving the public more efficiently. This rapid evolution—from struggling with basic math problems in 2020 to competing at the International Math Olympiad level by 2025, even achieving gold medal performance—shows how fast AI is advancing. As we gather at this National Day Rally—a moment to reflect on our progress and chart our future—it’s clear that AI is no longer theoretical or far-off. It matters to every Singaporean today and will shape our future.

Artificial intelligence will be a defining technology of our era. Singapore must harness it to raise productivity, strengthen competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all Singaporeans.

AI is already transforming how we work and live. Our strength lies in combining technology with human expertise—that’s what attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs. Look at Tuas Port: AI is integrated with robotics and autonomous machines to run operations more efficiently. At Changi Airport, AI is being studied for automation to improve processes. This isn’t just for the big players; SMEs are benefiting too. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components and alerts human operators for precise diagnosis—helping them catch issues early and save time. Even smaller companies like Q&M Dental use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately, improving detection of fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay.

AI adoption isn’t limited to large corporations; SMEs can gain real advantages as well.

But when new technology reshapes industries, the question always comes up: what about jobs?

This concern is valid and must be faced head-on. History shows us that technological change disrupts work—but it also creates new opportunities and better jobs over time. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills, transforming the economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, Singapore adopted it fully—and that propelled us forward again.

At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely supervise and manage multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Technological change shakes things up but enables workers to move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do so again in the age of artificial intelligence.

Our approach remains people-centred. The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills needed for this shift. We’re expanding job matching services, running more career fairs at tertiary institutions, and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils—to connect Singaporeans with opportunities closer to home.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is also expanding—it now covers more part-time training allowance claims and includes additional courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

For Singaporeans aged 40 and above, SkillsFuture credits provide up to $4,000 plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: at 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he continues his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

These efforts form a comprehensive package—supporting workers through job redesign, skills upgrading, and access to new career paths.

Building an economy that remains dynamic means creating good jobs that improve lives across all of society. Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—not by following trends blindly but by leading through ideas and innovation grounded in our strengths.

Singapore aims not just to keep pace but to stay ahead—through unity of purpose and clear focus on what drives real progress: people’s skills, adaptability, and opportunities.

Thank you.
