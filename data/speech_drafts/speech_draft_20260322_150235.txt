Have you ever wondered how secure Singapore’s energy supply truly is, especially given the ongoing conflicts around the world? This question is particularly relevant because Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas. Our heavy dependence on imported energy, especially natural gas, exposes us to significant risks. For instance, the recent conflicts in the Middle East have caused global liquefied natural gas prices to rise sharply, which has directly increased electricity costs here at home. This situation highlights our vulnerability to external disruptions and the tangible impact on both businesses and households. Having established this vulnerability, let us now examine how Singapore remains resilient despite these challenges.

Singapore mitigates these risks through a deliberate strategy of diversification and robust infrastructure development. We import natural gas from multiple countries—including Indonesia and Malaysia—via pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. Beyond piped gas, our LNG imports are diversified, with Australia serving as a major supplier among others. This diversification reduces our reliance on any single region, thereby lowering supply risks significantly. Moreover, we have invested in strong infrastructure; notably, a second LNG terminal is planned to increase capacity and provide a critical buffer against short-term supply shocks. These measures collectively ensure that disruptions in one area do not compromise Singapore’s overall energy stability. Looking ahead, Singapore is also transitioning towards a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Central to this transition is Singapore’s commitment to importing low-carbon electricity and developing alternative energy sources that will enhance future security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30 percent of projected demand—through large-scale renewable projects such as the Australia-Asia PowerLink and bilateral agreements with countries including Indonesia and Vietnam. Alongside these imports, Singapore has formulated a National Hydrogen Strategy and is investing substantially in research and development to integrate hydrogen as a key component of our future energy mix. In addition, ongoing efforts to improve energy efficiency across sectors help reduce overall demand growth. Together, these initiatives position Singapore not only to meet increasing energy needs but also to reduce carbon emissions responsibly. But it is important to recognize that energy security depends not only on infrastructure and technology; it also hinges on the actions of individuals and society.

Public participation plays an essential role in strengthening our national resilience. In 2023, households accounted for approximately 14.5 percent of Singapore’s total electricity consumption—a significant share that underscores the impact of everyday energy use on national demand. Energy security is therefore a shared responsibility involving both households and businesses through conscious conservation efforts. National campaigns such as Go Green SG actively promote awareness of responsible energy usage and encourage practices that reduce peak demand and overall consumption. By fostering this collective effort, these initiatives help alleviate pressure on supply systems during critical periods and enhance our overall resilience. Finally, we must consider how Singapore balances security, sustainability, and cost within its energy policies.

This balance—often referred to as the “energy trilemma”—presents a complex policy challenge: ensuring reliable energy supply while advancing environmental objectives and maintaining affordability for consumers. Singapore’s carbon tax policy exemplifies this tension; recent increases designed to promote sustainability are projected to raise household utility bills by a measurable margin in the near term. Navigating these competing priorities demands careful long-term planning amid persistent global uncertainties related to market dynamics and geopolitical developments. Only through sustained strategic commitment can we preserve energy security without compromising economic competitiveness or environmental responsibilities. In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security remains a dynamic achievement requiring strategic foresight, technological innovation, and collective effort.

To summarize: Singapore ensures its energy security through strategic diversification of supply sources, investment in innovative low-carbon technologies, development of resilient infrastructure, and active public participation in conservation efforts. Each one of us has an important role in sustaining this security for the nation’s future; together we must continue to engage with these challenges thoughtfully as circumstances evolve. Thank you.
