Good morning everyone.

Let’s start with something striking. Just five years ago, in 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems. Today, in 2025, it can compete at the International Math Olympiad—and not just compete, but perform at a gold medal level. That’s a huge leap in a short time. AI isn’t some distant technology anymore. It’s right here in Singapore, woven into our daily lives. When you plan your next trip, AI can recommend destinations and even craft personalized itineraries for you. In our civil service, AI helps by transcribing calls in all four official languages and summarizing conversations so officers can focus on what really matters—serving you better. And don’t forget healthcare: AI is already part of national screening programs for diabetes-related eye diseases across our public health system.

AI has transformed dramatically in just a few years. It’s no longer just a tool; it’s a powerful partner integrated into everyday activities. This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and innovation across Singapore’s economy.

Now, let’s talk about what this means for business and jobs.

Across sectors, AI is combining with robotics and autonomous machines to change how we work. At Tuas Port, this blend is already improving operations, and Changi Airport is studying automation possibilities to stay ahead. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts—alerting human operators for precise checks before problems escalate. It’s not just big players benefiting: SMEs like Q&M Dental harness AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings or tooth decay that might have been missed before.

Global giants like BHP, Oracle, and PwC have chosen Singapore as their base for new AI hubs. Why? Because we offer a rare mix of cutting-edge technology and skilled people ready to innovate together. This blend attracts investments that create good jobs—not just for large companies but also for small and medium enterprises.

But here’s the catch: while AI opens doors, it also raises real concerns about jobs being displaced. That’s something we must face head-on.

Looking back helps us see the way forward.

In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerization drive that taught students and workers computer skills—transforming our economy completely. At PSA today, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; instead, they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity and making their work more skilled and satisfying.

Take the example of Auntie Anne’s marketing employee who switched careers through Workforce Singapore's Career Conversion Programmes. She retrained as a Customer Retention Specialist who now analyzes data from new CRM systems—a clear upgrade from her previous role.

Technological change has always been disruptive at first—but history shows it ultimately pushes workers into higher-skilled, better-paying roles. Building on this history, the government is prioritizing support for Singaporeans to adapt and thrive in the AI era.

That support comes through concrete programs designed to help every worker succeed.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to two years. Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is one example: at 41 in 2025, he shifted from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer by using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he’s continuing studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

On another front, NTUC has pushed over 70 projects focused on AI through the Company Training Committee Grant—helping roughly 10,000 workers gain new skills relevant to today’s economy.

The government works closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with fresh skills. Job matching services are expanding; career fairs are connecting people with opportunities; financial assistance supports mid-career training—all aimed at making sure no one gets left behind as AI reshapes work.

With these efforts underway, Singapore is ready—not just to survive but to thrive—as we embrace AI for a dynamic and competitive future.

So where does this leave us?

Singapore's National AI Strategy 2.0 sets a clear vision: "AI for the Public Good—for Singapore and the World." It focuses on building peaks of excellence while empowering people and businesses alike.

To fuel innovation further, we’re investing over S$1 billion in public AI research between 2025 and 2030—a serious commitment that shows we’re all in on this future.

And here’s some validation: according to a 2025 Salesforce study, Singapore ranks second globally in AI readiness—and leads the world when it comes to AI governance.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay competitive on the global stage; it’s essential.

Singapore aims not only to keep pace but to lead—through ideas, innovation, and unity.

Thank you.
