Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—was something distant, almost sci-fi. Today, it’s right here, woven into our daily lives in ways many of us hardly notice. Think about planning your next trip: AI recommends destinations and even crafts personalized itineraries just for you. Or consider how our civil service uses AI in agency call centres—it transcribes conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries so officers can focus on serving you better. 

This rapid shift—from AI struggling with simple math problems in 2020 to competing at a gold medal level in the 2025 International Math Olympiad—is nothing short of remarkable. At this National Day Rally, a moment when we reflect on our progress and chart our future, AI isn’t just a tech story; it’s a story about every Singaporean’s future.

Artificial intelligence will define this era. And Singapore must harness it—not just to keep pace—but to boost productivity, sharpen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all of us.

Let’s put this into context. Back in 2020, AI couldn’t reliably solve basic math problems. Fast forward five years: it’s competing at the highest levels internationally. Meanwhile, AI is already part of everyday life here—from travel apps many use daily to government agencies where AI transcribes calls across multiple languages and summarizes them so officers can serve you more efficiently.

So AI is no longer some distant idea or theory. The real question for Singapore is: how do we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a world that’s changing fast?

Singapore’s edge lies in combining technology with human expertise—a mix that attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs. And this isn’t just about big corporations.

Look at Tuas Port—they’re blending AI with robotics and autonomous machines to transform operations. At Changi Airport, they’re studying automation powered by AI to improve efficiency. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine parts—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses.

And don’t overlook the small players: SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—to spot fillings, impacted teeth, and decay that might otherwise be missed.

AI adoption isn’t just for the giants—it’s an opportunity for all businesses, big or small.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, there’s a natural question: what will happen to jobs?

That’s a fair question—and one we have to face head-on.

History shows us that technological change disrupts—but it also creates new opportunities. It enables workers to move into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

Take the 1980s: Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—and transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate; we embraced it fully and propelled ourselves forward.

At PSA, crane operators were retrained not just to operate cranes but to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Technological shifts shake things up—they always do—but they also open doors for workers to move up into better jobs.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same now—with artificial intelligence.

This time around, our approach will be people-centred—no doubt about it.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this era. We’re expanding job matching services—with more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils (CDCs). This means more Singaporeans can find meaningful jobs closer to home.

Our SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is expanding too: more part-time training allowance claims are now supported, along with additional courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

Consider Vinoth Nanda Kumaran—41 years old in 2025—who shifted from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer. He used SkillsFuture credits first for a diploma in data engineering—and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

All these efforts connect back to one thing: building an economy that stays dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy doesn’t just create jobs—it creates good jobs that improve lives across the board—not just for some but for all of us.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—it keeps us ahead through ideas and innovation.

But there’s more at stake than competitiveness alone. Staying ahead means holding together as one people—united in purpose—to lead rather than follow.

So here’s the bottom line: AI is not some distant future threat or abstract concept. It’s here now—and how we use it will shape our economy and society going forward.

We need everyone on board—not just policymakers or businesses—but every Singaporean ready to adapt, learn new skills, and seize new opportunities.

Because if we don’t harness AI effectively today—if we don’t get this right—we risk falling behind in an unforgiving global race.

And I’m confident we won’t let that happen—not on my watch, not on yours either.

Thank you.
