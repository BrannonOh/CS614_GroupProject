Good evening, everyone.

Let me start with a quick story. Not long ago, I was planning a trip overseas. I used a simple AI travel planner that quickly sorted out flights, hotels, and even suggested the best local eateries. Or take a language translation app — it’s so good now that it almost feels like having a personal interpreter in your pocket. We barely notice these tools anymore because they’ve become part of our daily lives. But here’s the thing: this is just the start of a massive wave of technological change.

To secure its future and stay competitive on the global stage, Singapore must embrace Artificial Intelligence across every part of our economy and society. We need to turn challenges like job displacement into opportunities for growth, innovation, and better lives for all Singaporeans.

Let’s be clear: we are entering a new era with AI. Just think back to 2020 — AI struggled with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2024, and Google DeepMind’s AlphaGeometry system can now solve problems at a gold-medal level in the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s not just progress; it’s a leap.

And this isn’t some distant future scenario. A survey shows that 8 in 10 Singaporeans are already using AI tools for personal tasks. Even within our civil service, AI is helping agency call centres transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generate summaries — freeing up officers to focus on serving you better.

The rapid advancement of Generative AI marks a pivotal shift. It’s changing how we live and work and reshaping the world economy fundamentally.

But this new era of AI isn’t just about personal convenience; it’s a powerful engine for our national economy. So, how do we harness it?

Singapore is already putting AI to work in many sectors. At Tuas Port, AI combines with robotics and autonomous machines to boost operations. Changi Airport is studying automation closely to improve efficiency.

Take GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here — they use AI to detect anomalies in turbine parts and alert human operators for precise diagnosis. Even smaller players benefit: Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay with greater precision.

Big names like Oracle, PwC, and Grab are investing billions into setting up AI hubs right here in Singapore. Why? Because Singapore knows how to blend technology with human expertise — that combo attracts quality investments and creates good jobs.

And don’t think this is just for large companies; SMEs stand to gain too.

But as we see these productivity gains, there’s an obvious question on many minds: what does this mean for our jobs?

It’s a valid concern. History shows us that technological change disrupts jobs but also creates new opportunities.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills — transforming our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it successfully and moved forward.

At PSA, crane operators were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously — boosting productivity while improving job quality.

That said, jobs involving routine tasks—like administration, customer service, and logistics—are most exposed to disruption from AI.

But there’s growing demand for new roles: AI Engineers, Machine Learning Specialists, Data Scientists — these are real jobs with real prospects expanding rapidly here.

Technological change has always been disruptive but also opened doors for workers to move into higher-skilled and better-paying roles.

History shows we can navigate these changes. But it requires deliberate effort — no one should be left behind. That is our promise.

Even as we embrace AI and technology, Singaporeans remain our key priority.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills.

Programs like the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provide Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 worth of SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances for full-time study.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example—at 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering and continuing studies at SUSS in Business Analytics.

Through NTUC’s Company Training Committees (CTCs), unions and management collaborate on transformation projects; notably, the number of AI-related projects more than doubled in 2025 compared to 2024.

Efforts also include expanded job matching services, more career fairs at tertiary institutions, plus a new town-level job matching initiative led by CDCs—all aimed at helping Singaporeans access good jobs amidst change.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will expand further to include more part-time training allowance claims and courses from industry leaders as well as private providers.

These individual and collective efforts are part of a much larger national strategy.

Embracing AI fits squarely within Singapore's broader goal: building a dynamic economy that creates good jobs and better lives for all citizens while staying globally competitive.

A recent Salesforce study ranked Singapore second worldwide in AI readiness — thanks to our strong regulatory foundation and digital infrastructure.

And it shows in paychecks too: the median annual salary for AI professionals here reached S$133,300 in 2025 — well above the national median — proving that high-value jobs are being created right now.

A dynamic economy means opportunity for everyone — powered by ideas, innovation—and unity among us all as Singaporeans striving together toward the future.

Thank you.
