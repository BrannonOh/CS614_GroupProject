Ladies and gentlemen,

It’s remarkable to witness how artificial intelligence has transformed in such a short time. Not long ago, AI struggled with tasks as basic as high school math. Yet, by 2025, experts predict that AI models will perform at a gold medal level in the International Math Olympiad—something once thought decades away. This rapid advancement is not just theoretical; AI has become a practical tool woven into the fabric of everyday life here in Singapore. Millions of us use AI daily, whether it’s through Google Maps’ smart routing that helps us avoid traffic jams on the Pan-Island Expressway or personalized recommendations on our streaming platforms. Even our public services harness AI to transcribe call centre conversations across English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil, allowing officers to focus more deeply on serving citizens. This rapid progress opens new possibilities for productivity and innovation across industries, which we will explore next.

Singapore’s approach to AI is strategic and inclusive. By augmenting human expertise with AI, we attract high-value investments that create specialized and future-proof jobs. But AI is not just the domain of large corporations; it’s a democratizing force empowering Small and Medium Enterprises to boost productivity and compete on a global scale. Take Tuas Port, for example—one of the world’s most advanced ports—where AI coordinates fleets of autonomous vehicles and cranes. This innovation drastically improves efficiency and safety while enabling human operators to manage operations from control centres rather than working directly amid heavy machinery. At GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility, technicians use AI-powered visual inspection tools to detect microscopic defects invisible to the naked eye, speeding up repairs with greater accuracy. And local SMEs like Q&M Dental employ AI systems that analyze X-rays to identify early-stage cavities with precision, resulting in better patient care. While AI brings many benefits, it also raises concerns about job displacement, which we must address honestly.

These concerns are understandable because history has shown that technological revolutions often spark anxiety about job loss. Yet experience teaches us that while some roles may change or disappear, new opportunities emerge—often higher-value ones—for those who adapt and upskill. In Singapore’s own history, the computerization drive of the 1980s didn’t eliminate clerical jobs; instead, it transformed them into administrative and IT support roles that helped build our modern services economy. Similarly, when the internet arrived in the 1990s, fears ran high about job security, but it ultimately created entire industries like e-commerce and digital media that now employ thousands. More recently at PSA, crane operators who once worked alone in cabins high above the port have been retrained as ‘AI supervisors,’ managing multiple automated cranes from comfortable control rooms—a role that offers higher pay and better working conditions. Building on this legacy, Singapore is committed to prioritising its people by supporting job redesign and skills development in the AI era.

To make this commitment real, Singapore has established a tripartite partnership between government agencies, unions, and employers focused on redesigning jobs and systematically upskilling workers for an AI-driven economy. The national SkillsFuture movement is a cornerstone of this effort, providing substantial support for mid-career workers looking to pivot into new roles where demand is growing fast. For example, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above a $4,000 credit top-up plus a monthly training allowance—making full-time career switches financially viable even later in life. This isn’t just theory: take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran’s story—a former private hire driver who retrained at SUSS using these resources and is now thriving as a Business Intelligence Developer in a high-demand field. Beyond individuals like Vinoth, over 140,000 workers have benefited from NTUC’s Company Training Committees that collaborate with companies to identify future skills needs and implement targeted training plans across industries. With these supports in place, Singapore is ready to embrace AI for a dynamic and competitive future.

Our vision goes beyond merely adopting artificial intelligence—we aim to master it so technology serves human ambition and drives shared prosperity for all Singaporeans. By investing as much in our people’s skills as we do in cutting-edge technology infrastructure, we ensure that Singapore remains not only a hub of opportunity but also a leader on the global stage. The AI revolution is not something to fear; rather it is a challenge we meet with unity, foresight, and courage—the very qualities that have always defined Singapore’s success.

Thank you.
