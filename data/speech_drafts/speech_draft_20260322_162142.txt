Ladies and gentlemen,

Energy security is a concern that resonates deeply with many Singaporeans today. Our nation depends heavily on imported energy, with about 95% of our electricity generated from natural gas brought in from abroad. This reliance exposes us to vulnerabilities, especially when global conflicts arise. Recent tensions in the Middle East, for instance, have disrupted supply chains and caused fluctuations in energy prices, underscoring how international developments can directly affect our domestic energy stability and economic well-being. These realities remind us that despite our geographic distance, Singapore remains exposed to global risks that challenge our energy security. Having established these risks, let us now examine how Singapore has built resilience against such challenges.

To reduce this vulnerability, Singapore has implemented strategic measures to diversify its energy sources and suppliers. We import Liquefied Natural Gas—or LNG—from a broad range of countries including the United States, Australia, and Qatar. This diversification mitigates dependence on any single region and helps buffer us from supply shocks caused by geopolitical tensions or market volatility. Supporting this strategy is strong infrastructure: the Singapore LNG Terminal, with a send-out capacity of 11 million tonnes per annum, along with strategic stockpiles, provides a critical buffer against short-term disruptions. Together, these efforts strengthen our capacity to maintain a stable energy supply amid external uncertainties. With this foundation of resilience in place, Singapore is now turning to innovation to secure its energy future.

Looking ahead, Singapore is investing significantly in renewable and low-carbon energy sources to enhance our long-term energy security. By 2035, we plan to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—expected to meet approximately 30% of our domestic demand—through regional grids that tap into solar and other renewable resources. To support this goal, we have formalized agreements with neighboring countries such as Indonesia to facilitate cross-border electricity trade. Moreover, our National Hydrogen Strategy, launched in 2022, targets low-carbon hydrogen to supply up to 50% of our power needs by 2050. These initiatives represent a pragmatic transition toward cleaner energy sources while maintaining reliability and affordability. However, it is important to recognize that energy security extends beyond infrastructure and technology—it requires active participation from all sectors of society.

Indeed, securing Singapore’s energy future involves not only government policies but also the collective actions of households and businesses. The residential sector accounts for approximately 14.9% of Singapore’s total energy consumption—a significant proportion where conservation efforts can make a meaningful impact. National initiatives such as Go Green SG and campaigns led by the National Environment Agency encourage households to adopt energy-saving habits that reduce overall demand. By building awareness and promoting responsible usage across all sectors, we strengthen national resilience against supply disruptions and price volatility. Energy security is therefore not solely a government responsibility; it is a shared commitment that depends on informed and conscientious behavior throughout society. Finally, let us consider how Singapore manages the complex balance between energy security, sustainability, and affordability.

Balancing a reliable energy supply with environmental objectives and cost considerations presents an ongoing challenge for policymakers in Singapore. Our ranking of 31st in the 2023 World Energy Trilemma Index reflects this complexity—it measures performance across three critical dimensions: security of supply, equity (or affordability), and sustainability. To align economic growth with climate commitments while maintaining competitiveness, Singapore will raise its carbon tax to S$45 per ton in 2026; this policy is designed explicitly to balance environmental goals with the costs borne by businesses and consumers. Through deliberate long-term planning and adaptive policy frameworks, we aim to sustain an energy system that remains secure even amid global uncertainties while supporting sustainable development for future generations. In conclusion, Singapore’s comprehensive approach—rooted in diversification, innovation, collective responsibility, and prudent policy—positions us well to navigate current challenges and those yet to come.

Thank you.
