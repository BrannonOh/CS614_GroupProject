Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—felt like something from a sci-fi story, distant and hard to grasp. Today, it’s right here with us, woven into our daily lives in ways many of us might not even notice. Think about planning your next trip—AI helps by recommending destinations and even creating personalized itineraries just for you. Or consider how our civil service uses AI in agency call centres: it transcribes conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries, so officers can spend more time helping you directly. This rapid shift—from AI struggling with simple math problems in 2020 to competing at a gold medal level in the 2025 International Math Olympiad—is nothing short of remarkable. At this National Day Rally, as we reflect on our progress and future, this matters to every Singaporean today and tomorrow.

Artificial intelligence will define this era. Singapore must harness it—not just to keep up—but to boost productivity, sharpen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all of us.

Let me put this into context. Back in 2020, AI couldn’t reliably solve basic math problems. Fast forward five years—it’s competing at the highest levels internationally. Meanwhile, AI is already part of everyday life here: from travel apps many use daily to government agencies where AI transcribes calls across multiple languages and summarizes them so officers can serve you more efficiently.

So AI is no longer some distant idea or theory. The real question for Singapore is: how do we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a world that’s changing fast?

Singapore’s edge lies in combining technology with human expertise—a mix that attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs. And this isn’t just about big corporations. Look at Tuas Port: they’re blending AI with robotics and autonomous machines to transform operations. At Changi Airport, they’re exploring automation powered by AI. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine parts—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses. Even smaller companies are joining in; Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—to spot fillings, impacted teeth, and decay.

AI adoption isn’t just for the giants—it’s an opportunity for all businesses.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, there’s a natural question: what will happen to jobs?

That’s a fair question—and one we have to face directly.

History tells us that technological change disrupts but also opens doors. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate; we adopted it fully and propelled ourselves forward.

Take PSA as an example: crane operators were retrained not just to operate cranes but to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Technological shifts aren’t easy—they shake things up—but they also create chances for workers to move into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again now—with artificial intelligence.

This time around, our approach will be people-centred.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this era. We’re expanding job matching services—with more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils (CDCs). These efforts make sure Singaporeans have access to meaningful jobs across the island.

Our SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is expanding too: more part-time training allowance claims are now supported, along with additional courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

For example, Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was 41 years old in 2025 when he transitioned from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer. He used SkillsFuture credits first to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative—and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs and better lives for all of us—not just some of us.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—it keeps us ahead through ideas and innovation.

More importantly, Singapore aims to remain ahead by combining these strengths with unity—a commitment from all of us working together as one people determined not just to adapt but to lead.

The future belongs to those who prepare for it today—and Singapore is ready.

Thank you.
