Good morning, everyone.

Look around you. AI isn’t some far-off future idea anymore. It’s already woven into the fabric of daily life here in Singapore. When you plan a trip, AI helps by recommending destinations and even crafting personalised itineraries tailored just for you. From booking your flights to navigating government services online, AI is quietly working behind the scenes, making things smoother and faster. We’re stepping into a new era—one where AI isn’t just a tool; it’s a part of how we live and work every day.

This growing presence of AI sets the stage for how it can further boost Singapore’s economy and competitiveness.

Now, what does that mean for us as a nation? The adoption of AI across different sectors isn’t just about convenience—it’s about raising productivity and creating new economic value. By integrating AI into our businesses and industries, we can work smarter, not just harder. This isn’t about replacing people; it’s about augmenting our capabilities to do more and do it better.

To understand this better, let’s look at concrete examples of AI applications in Singapore’s industries.

Take Tuas Port, for instance. There, AI teams up with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations on a massive scale. Over at Changi Airport, researchers are exploring how automation powered by AI can improve efficiency and safety. Then there’s GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility right here in Singapore—using AI to spot anomalies in turbine components that human eyes might miss, alerting operators so they can diagnose issues precisely before they escalate. Even small and medium enterprises are on board: Q&M Dental uses AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately, detecting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay with greater precision than ever before.

While AI brings many benefits, it also raises concerns about job displacement that we must address.

I won’t sugarcoat this: technological change shakes things up. But history shows us that disruption also creates opportunities. When PSA introduced automation at their ports, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs—they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once. This shift boosted productivity and improved job quality by moving workers into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

Building on these lessons, the government is actively supporting Singaporeans through this AI-driven transition.

We’re not leaving anyone behind. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is one example—offering Singaporeans aged 40 and above up to $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to two years. Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is a good example: at 41 in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. Beyond training, we’re expanding job matching services with more career fairs at tertiary institutions and launching new town-level job matching initiatives led by Community Development Councils. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will also grow to include more part-time training allowances and courses from industry leaders and private providers.

With these supports in place, Singapore is poised to embrace AI for a dynamic and competitive future.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just some of us. Embracing AI is crucial if we want to stay competitive on the global stage. But it’s not just about technology or business—it’s about ideas, innovation, and unity among all of us here in Singapore that will keep us ahead.

Thank you.
