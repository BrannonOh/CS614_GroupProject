Good evening, everyone.

Let’s start with a clear observation: AI has come a long way in just a few years. Back in 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems—it couldn’t solve them reliably. Fast forward to 2025, and AI is not just solving problems; it’s competing in the International Math Olympiad and performing at a gold medal level. That’s not some distant future—this rapid evolution is happening right now.

And it’s not just about math. AI is already part of everyday life for Singaporeans. Planning your next trip? AI tools recommend destinations and generate personalised itineraries tailored to you. In our civil service, an AI tool called Transcribe works quietly in agency call centres. It transcribes conversations in all four official languages—and yes, even Singlish—and creates summaries so officers can focus on what matters most: serving the public efficiently.

This dramatic transformation means AI has become woven into daily experiences here in Singapore. It’s no longer a novelty; it’s a tool changing how we live and work. This rapid progress sets the stage for how AI can boost productivity and innovation across our economy.

Now, let’s get into the heart of it. Across industries, AI teams up with robotics and autonomous machines at Tuas Port, improving efficiency and cutting costs. Changi Airport is actively exploring automation to streamline operations and enhance passenger experience. Over at GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here in Singapore, AI detects anomalies in turbine components early and alerts human operators for precise diagnosis—catching problems before they become expensive failures.

But this isn’t just about big companies. SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, tooth decay—to help dentists provide better care. The benefits of AI aren’t limited to giants; they’re accessible to small businesses too.

Singapore is backing this momentum with serious investments: over S$1 billion in public AI research from 2025 to 2030. On top of that, billions more have flowed in from private investors like Google, Microsoft, and Alibaba Cloud. They see Singapore as a place where technology meets human expertise—a unique blend that attracts quality investments and creates good jobs for all.

But let’s be frank: while AI drives growth, it also brings real challenges—especially job displacement—that we cannot ignore or sugarcoat.

Technological change always shakes things up. But history shows us how disruption can lead to opportunity. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy fundamentally.

Take PSA as an example. Crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; instead, they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Between 1981 and 1995, productivity growth accounted for 60% of Singapore's average economic growth of 7.6%. Change isn’t easy—but it opens doors to higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

Building on this track record, Singapore is prioritising support so workers can thrive through the AI transition—not be left behind.

That means action today: The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme offers Singaporeans aged 40 and above up to $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to two years.

Consider Vinoth Nanda Kumaran—a perfect example of what’s possible. At 41 years old in 2025, he moved from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and he continues his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

Beyond individuals like Vinoth, NTUC’s Company Training Committees bring union leaders together with company management to plan business transformation hand-in-hand. There are grants specifically supporting AI projects tied directly to job redesign and skills training.

Expanded job matching services and career fairs are helping people find new opportunities faster than ever before.

The government isn’t leaving anyone behind—it works closely with unions and businesses to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills for tomorrow’s economy.

With these concrete efforts underway, Singapore is ready—not just willing—to embrace AI for an inclusive future that benefits every citizen.

Looking ahead—the stakes couldn’t be higher—and so are the rewards. According to projections from the Economic Development Board report on strengthening Singapore’s AI leadership with Google, adopting AI here could add up to S$198.3 billion in economic benefits by 2030.

That kind of economic strength means good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans who seize these opportunities.

But make no mistake: ignoring AI isn’t an option if we want to stay globally competitive. We must lead—not follow—through ideas, innovation—and unity as one nation ready for what lies ahead.

Thank you.
