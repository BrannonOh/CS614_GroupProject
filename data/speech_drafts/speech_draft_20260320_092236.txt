Ladies and gentlemen,

Just a few years ago, artificial intelligence was mostly a distant concept—something you’d hear about in research labs or see in sci-fi movies. Today, AI has exploded into everyday life here in Singapore. In 2025, AI models from Google and OpenAI didn’t just perform well—they scored gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s a huge jump in capability in a short time. But it’s not just about impressive feats on the global stage. Right now, Singaporeans use AI to plan their travels with personalized recommendations that make trips smoother and more efficient. Our civil service is already using AI to transcribe calls in all four official languages and produce summaries that help officers serve you faster and better. AI has gone from a niche tool to something we live with daily.

This rapid progress shows AI isn’t some far-off future anymore; it’s here now. So let’s look at how Singapore can put this technology to work for growth.

Across our economy, AI is making real changes. At Tuas Port, robotics and autonomous machines powered by AI are speeding up how goods move through one of the busiest ports in the world. Over at Changi Airport, they’re exploring automation powered by AI to keep operations running smoothly. GE Vernova uses AI to spot turbine problems early so repairs are exact and efficient—cutting downtime and costs. And it’s not just big players benefiting: small businesses like Q&M Dental use AI to analyze dental X-rays for better diagnosis and patient care. This isn’t just tech for tech’s sake—it’s a smart mix of human skills and AI tools that attracts investments and creates good jobs across the board, from large firms right down to SMEs.

Singapore is backing this up with serious investment—over S$1 billion committed to public AI research, plus private funding that helped us grab 68% of ASEAN’s AI deal value in the first half of 2024 alone.

Seeing these examples makes one thing clear: AI brings real benefits—but it also raises tough questions about jobs and workers.

Let’s call it what it is: technological change shakes things up—and yes, that means some jobs disappear or change shape. But disruption isn’t new here. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a computerisation drive that didn’t just digitize tasks; it equipped workers with new skills that transformed our economy from manufacturing to services. Then came the internet boom in the 1990s—that pushed us even further ahead, opening new industries and opportunities.

Look at PSA today: crane operators didn’t lose their jobs when automation arrived—they were retrained to remotely manage multiple cranes at once. That raised productivity *and* improved job quality by giving them more control and less physical strain.

Experts warn that roles like data entry, administrative support, and basic coding face the highest risk from AI disruption here in Singapore—no sugarcoating that fact.

But here’s what history teaches us: disruption also creates room for workers to move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

That track record of adapting sets a clear path for how we can handle this next wave of change—with people front and center.

The government isn’t sitting on its hands either. We’re working closely with unions to redesign jobs so they fit this new reality—and we’re equipping workers with fresh skills through expanded training programs.

Take SkillsFuture Level-Up: it offers up to $4,000 in credits plus $3,000 monthly allowances for full-time study—especially aimed at Singaporeans aged 40 and above who want to upgrade their skills. And this programme is expanding, opening doors for even more people.

Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is one example among many: he went from being a driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer by tapping into SkillsFuture credits and further studies—a clear sign that transformation is possible when opportunity meets effort.

In 2025 alone, over 105,000 people joined SkillsFuture-supported AI training programs—a strong signal that Singaporeans are stepping up.

And beyond training? We’re broadening job matching services, hosting career fairs focused on emerging sectors, and rolling out initiatives right down at the town level—all aimed at making sure no one gets left behind.

These efforts show Singapore is serious about making sure AI works for all of us as our economy evolves.

Looking ahead, Singapore’s updated National Artificial Intelligence Strategy—NAIS 2.0—isn’t just about keeping pace with global trends. It focuses on building centres of expertise in AI while giving people and businesses the confidence—and tools—to adopt this technology effectively and responsibly.

This strategy rests on three key pillars: developing peaks of excellence in research and development; empowering businesses to innovate with AI; and ensuring people have access to opportunities through skills upgrading and job support.

The results speak volumes: thanks largely to booming global demand for AI-related products, we’ve revised our economic growth forecast for 2026 upward—to between 2.0 and 4.0 percent.

A dynamic economy means good jobs—and better lives—for every Singaporean.

If we want to stay competitive on the world stage—and keep creating those good jobs—we have no choice but to embrace AI thoughtfully and inclusively.

Singapore won’t just follow trends; we aim to lead through practical ideas grounded in real results—and by sticking together as one community ready for change.

Thank you.
