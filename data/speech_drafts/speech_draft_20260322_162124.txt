Ladies and gentlemen,

In today’s interconnected world, energy security has become a pressing concern for many, especially here in Singapore. Our nation depends heavily on imported energy to power our homes and industries—about 95% of our electricity is generated from natural gas brought in from abroad. This reliance exposes us to vulnerabilities, particularly when global conflicts arise. Recent tensions in the Middle East, for example, have threatened both the stability of supply and the prices we pay for energy. Such disruptions remind us that while we may be geographically distant, we are not insulated from global risks that can affect our daily lives and economic well-being. Having established these risks, let us now explore how Singapore has built resilience against these challenges.

To mitigate these vulnerabilities, Singapore has implemented several strategic measures aimed at reducing dependency on any single source or region. We import Liquefied Natural Gas—or LNG—from a diverse range of countries including the United States, Australia, and Qatar. This diversification is critical in buffering us from supply shocks that might arise from geopolitical tensions or market fluctuations. Complementing this approach is our robust infrastructure: the Singapore LNG Terminal boasts a send-out capacity of 11 million tonnes per annum, supported by strategic stockpiles that provide a buffer against short-term disruptions. These efforts collectively enhance our ability to maintain a stable energy supply despite external uncertainties. With this foundation of resilience, Singapore is now looking ahead to secure its energy future through innovation.

Looking forward, Singapore is actively investing in renewable and low-carbon energy sources to further strengthen our energy security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—enough to meet approximately 30% of our domestic demand—through regional grids tapping into solar and other renewable resources. To facilitate this, we have signed agreements with neighboring countries such as Indonesia to enable cross-border electricity trade. Additionally, our National Hydrogen Strategy, launched in 2022, sets an ambitious target for low-carbon hydrogen to supply up to half of our power needs by 2050. These initiatives represent a pragmatic shift toward cleaner energy sources while maintaining reliability and affordability. But energy security is not just about infrastructure and technology—it requires everyone’s participation.

Indeed, securing Singapore’s energy future extends beyond government policies and large-scale projects; it involves each one of us as individuals and members of society. The residential sector accounts for nearly 15% of the nation’s total energy consumption—a significant share that households can influence through conservation efforts. National initiatives like Go Green SG and campaigns led by the National Environment Agency encourage households to adopt energy-saving habits that collectively reduce demand and strengthen national resilience. Energy security is not solely a government responsibility; it depends on building awareness and fostering responsible usage across all sectors. Finally, let us consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and cost to ensure a stable future.

Balancing reliable energy supply with environmental goals and affordability presents an ongoing challenge for Singapore’s policymakers. Our ranking of 31st in the 2023 World Energy Trilemma Index reflects the complexity of managing these three dimensions simultaneously: security, equity, and sustainability. To align economic growth with climate commitments, Singapore will raise its carbon tax to S$45 per ton in 2026—a key policy tool designed to incentivize cleaner practices without compromising competitiveness or access to affordable energy. Through careful long-term planning and adaptive strategies, we aim to maintain a secure energy system capable of weathering global uncertainties while supporting sustainable development for generations ahead. In conclusion, Singapore’s comprehensive approach positions it well to face current and future energy challenges.

Thank you.
