Tonight, I want to speak to a concern that many Singaporeans share deeply: our energy security in an increasingly uncertain world. Singapore imports over 95% of its electricity needs through natural gas, which means we rely heavily on supplies from abroad. This dependence exposes us to vulnerabilities—especially when conflicts arise in critical regions such as the Middle East. Such conflicts can disrupt global energy availability and cause prices to rise sharply, directly impacting our economy and the cost of living here at home. This is not a distant problem; it is a shared challenge that affects all of us.

Having understood these risks, let us now examine how Singapore maintains resilience despite these challenges.

Singapore’s energy security strategy is built on deliberate diversification and robust infrastructure to reduce vulnerability. We import natural gas from multiple countries, including Indonesia and Malaysia, using both pipelines and liquefied natural gas shipments. This diversification reduces our reliance on any single supplier or region, thereby mitigating risk. Our LNG terminal, with a peak send-out capacity of around 11 million tonnes per annum, provides a significant buffer against short-term supply shocks. Alongside this, strategic reserves further strengthen our ability to manage fluctuations in the global energy market and ensure supply stability.

Building on this resilience, Singapore is also planning for a more secure and sustainable energy future.

Looking ahead, Singapore is investing strategically in low-carbon energy sources to enhance long-term energy security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—approximately 30% of our projected demand. A key initiative supporting this goal is the Lao PDR-Thailand-Malaysia-Singapore Power Integration Project, or LTMS-PIP. This project represents ASEAN’s first multilateral cross-border electricity trade and enables the import of renewable energy such as solar power from regional grids. In addition to these imports, we are developing low-carbon alternatives like hydrogen and advancing improvements in energy efficiency across sectors to reduce overall consumption and emissions.

While technology and policy are vital components of our strategy, the role of individuals and society is equally important.

Energy security depends not only on government action but also on active participation from households and businesses through conservation efforts. In 2023, households accounted for about 8 terawatt hours of electricity consumption—roughly 14.5% of the national total. This means that responsible energy use at home can make a significant difference in strengthening national resilience. Raising awareness about energy conservation and promoting responsible consumption are critical steps that empower each of us to contribute meaningfully during times when supply may be constrained.

Finally, let us consider how Singapore balances energy security with sustainability and affordability.

Our policy approach must carefully navigate three interconnected priorities: ensuring reliable supply, achieving environmental sustainability, and maintaining affordability for consumers and businesses alike. For example, Singapore’s carbon tax policy—which will increase to S$45 per ton by 2026—is designed to encourage emissions reductions while balancing economic impacts prudently. We recognize that this balancing act involves complexity; some may be concerned about rising costs or potential disruptions as we transition toward cleaner energy sources. However, comprehensive long-term planning remains essential for managing global uncertainties—from geopolitical tensions to technological changes—and securing an energy future that supports both economic growth and environmental commitments.

In conclusion, Singapore’s energy security remains strong despite global disruptions because of strategic diversification across suppliers and infrastructure; forward-looking investments in low-carbon technologies; active public participation in conservation; and balanced policy planning that integrates reliability, sustainability, and affordability. It is through this comprehensive approach that we can confidently address the challenges posed by an uncertain world.

Thank you for your attention.
