Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Look around you. Artificial intelligence used to be something from science fiction or distant tech reports. Now, it’s right here—in the apps we use to plan our holidays, recommending destinations and crafting personalised itineraries just for us. It’s in the public services we rely on every day. In our civil service call centres, AI transcribes conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries. That frees up officers to focus on serving you better. This isn’t some far-off future. It’s happening now, shaping how we live and work in Singapore.

At this National Day Rally—a moment for reflection and progress—we have to be clear: artificial intelligence is no longer a distant idea. It is a defining technology of our era. Back in 2020, AI struggled even with basic math problems. Fast forward to 2025, and AI competes at the International Math Olympiad—performing at a gold medal level. That leap shows how quickly AI is advancing and why it demands our attention.

So here’s the big idea: Artificial intelligence will define this era, and Singapore must harness it to raise productivity, strengthen competitiveness, and create better opportunities for every Singaporean.

The question now is how we do that.

Take Tuas Port. There, AI works hand in hand with robotics and autonomous machines to speed up operations and boost efficiency—making the port more competitive globally. At Changi Airport, AI-driven automation is already deployed to improve processes and passenger experiences—not just studied but in action. Even GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to spot anomalies in turbine parts early, alerting human experts who then diagnose issues precisely. This blend of machine accuracy and human skill is what gives Singapore an edge.

And don’t think this is only for big companies. Small and medium enterprises are getting on board too. Q&M Dental uses AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, tooth decay—that might otherwise be missed. This helps dentists make better decisions and gives patients better care.

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise—that mix attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs with real career paths.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, people ask: what about jobs?

Let’s not dodge that question.

Technology always disrupts existing roles. But history shows us something important: new technology doesn’t just take jobs away—it creates new ones that are higher-skilled and better paid.

In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—and transformed our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it fast—and that propelled us forward.

Today at PSA, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity while making jobs safer and less physically demanding.

We supported workers through those past transformations—and we must do the same again now with artificial intelligence.

Our approach will be people-centred from start to finish.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs so workers can thrive alongside new technologies—not be left behind.

We’re expanding job matching services: more career fairs at tertiary institutions; a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils—all designed to help Singaporeans find jobs that fit their skills and ambitions.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will also grow—offering more part-time training allowances; adding courses from industry leaders and private training providers—to help workers keep their skills sharp.

This programme already provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to 24 months of full-time study.

Consider Vinoth Nanda Kumaran’s story: At 41 years old in 2025, he moved from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

Vinoth’s journey shows what happens when support meets opportunity—the SkillsFuture programme made his transition possible.

Ultimately, these efforts are about building an economy that stays dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all of us.

Embracing AI isn’t just about keeping up; it’s about staying ahead globally through ideas, innovation—and unity as a nation.

Some say AI will leave people behind or make us dependent on machines. I say that depends on the choices we make together now—not on some inevitable future handed down by technology itself.

Singapore won’t just keep pace—we aim to lead by making smart choices grounded in reality—and by standing together as one people ready for what comes next.

Thank you.
