Let’s start by looking at how quickly artificial intelligence has changed. In 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems—often getting them wrong. Fast forward to 2025, and AI can compete at the International Math Olympiad, performing at a gold medal level. That’s a huge leap in just five years. Here in Singapore, AI is no longer a distant idea; it’s part of our daily lives. Our civil service uses AI in agency call centres to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and create summaries, freeing officers to focus on helping you better. A 2025 study found that the use of generative AI among Singaporean travellers for planning trips nearly doubled—from 11% to 20% in one year. This rapid progress shows how AI has transformed into a powerful tool woven into everyday activities. By embracing artificial intelligence thoughtfully and inclusively, Singapore can stay competitive, create good jobs, and build a dynamic future for all its people. This rapid progress opens new opportunities for Singapore’s economy and workforce, which we will explore next.

Across sectors in Singapore, AI adoption is boosting productivity and creating value for businesses of all sizes. At Tuas Port, AI works alongside robotics and autonomous machines through the Next Generation Vessel Traffic Management System to optimize ship movements and berth allocation efficiently. Over at Changi Airport, AI-powered autonomous tractors transport baggage on the airside, raising manpower productivity significantly. Even small businesses benefit: Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay with greater precision. Major tech companies are betting on Singapore too; Amazon Web Services plans to invest an additional $9 billion here from 2024 to 2029 alone. This mix of technology and human expertise attracts investments and creates quality jobs—not just for big companies but also for SMEs. These gains are real—but with growth come concerns about jobs. Let’s address those next.

It’s natural to worry about what new technology means for work. But history gives us perspective—and some reassurance. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy in the process. Then in the 1990s came Singapore ONE—one of the world’s first nationwide broadband networks—which pushed us ahead into the digital age before many others caught up. Technological change has always caused disruption at first but ultimately helped workers move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles. That pattern holds true with AI as well. Building on this history, the government is committed to supporting Singaporeans through skills development and job access.

This commitment is clear in action today. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: at 41 in 2025, he transitioned from private hire driver to Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and he continues his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS now. The government works closely with unions through NTUC’s Company Training Committees to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for today’s economy. There are expanded job matching services, career fairs, and town-level initiatives helping people find new opportunities as well. Importantly, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is expanding—the Mid-Career Training Allowance now supports part-time learners for the first time too. With these supports firmly in place, Singapore is ready to face AI head-on for a competitive future.

Looking ahead bluntly: AI will change how we work—and those who don’t adapt risk being left behind. But if we get this right together, AI adoption is projected to add up to S$198.3 billion to Singapore’s economy by 2030—a massive boost that can create good jobs and better lives across our society—not just for some but for all who call this country home. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay globally competitive; it’s essential. And Singapore aims not just to keep pace but to lead—through ideas, innovation, and unity as one nation meeting these changes together.

Thank you.
