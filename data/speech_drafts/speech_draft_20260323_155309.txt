Let’s take a moment to look at how fast artificial intelligence has evolved. Back in 2020, AI struggled to solve even basic math problems reliably. Five years later, in 2025, it reached a level where it could compete—and win gold—at the International Math Olympiad. That’s a huge leap in a short time. Right here in Singapore, AI is no longer just a concept; it’s part of our everyday lives. Our civil service uses AI in agency call centres to transcribe conversations in all four official languages and generate summaries, so officers can spend more time helping you. A recent 2025 study showed that generative AI use among Singaporean travellers for planning trips nearly doubled—from 11% to 20% in just one year. This rapid evolution shows how AI has become a powerful tool woven into daily activities. This progress opens new doors for Singapore’s economy and workforce, which we’ll explore next.

Across Singapore, AI is boosting productivity and creating value across industries big and small. At Tuas Port, AI works with robotics and autonomous machines through the “Next Generation Vessel Traffic Management System” to optimize ship movements and berth allocation efficiently. Over at Changi Airport, AI-powered autonomous tractors handle baggage transport on the airside, lifting manpower productivity significantly. Even small businesses are getting in on this: Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay with greater precision. Big tech companies are betting on Singapore too; Amazon Web Services alone plans to invest an extra $9 billion here between 2024 and 2029. This mix of technology and human skill attracts investments and creates quality jobs across the board—not just for large firms but for SMEs as well. While these gains are real, we know AI also stirs worries about jobs. Let’s tackle that next.

It’s understandable to be concerned about what new technology means for work. But history offers some perspective. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with essential computer skills—transforming our economy along the way. Then in the 1990s came Singapore ONE—one of the world’s first nationwide broadband networks—which pushed us ahead into the digital age before many others caught up. Technological shifts have always disrupted jobs at first but ultimately helped workers move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles. That’s the pattern we expect with AI too. Building on this track record, the government is committed to helping Singaporeans through skills development and job access.

This commitment is already clear in action today. The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme gives Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months. Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran: at 41 in 2025, he shifted from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he continues his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS now. The government works closely with unions through NTUC’s Company Training Committees to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for today’s economy. There are also expanded job matching services, career fairs, and initiatives right down to town level helping people find new opportunities. Importantly, the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is expanding—the Mid-Career Training Allowance now supports part-time learners for the first time as well. With these supports firmly in place, Singapore is ready to face AI head-on for a competitive future.

Looking ahead, let me be blunt: AI will change how we work—and those who don’t adapt risk being left behind. But if we get this right together, AI adoption is projected to add up to S$198.3 billion to Singapore’s economy by 2030—a massive boost that can create good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just some of us but everyone who calls this country home. Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay competitive on the global stage; it’s essential. And Singapore aims not just to keep pace but to lead—through ideas, innovation, and unity as one nation facing these changes together.

Thank you.
