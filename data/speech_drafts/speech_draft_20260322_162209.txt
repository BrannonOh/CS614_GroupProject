Ladies and gentlemen,

Energy security is a pressing concern for many Singaporeans, particularly in light of ongoing global conflicts that disrupt supply chains. Singapore depends heavily on imported energy, with approximately 95% of our electricity generated from natural gas sourced abroad. This significant reliance exposes us to vulnerabilities, especially when geopolitical tensions arise in critical regions such as the Middle East. Such conflicts not only threaten the physical availability of energy supplies but also contribute to price volatility, directly impacting our economy and households. These realities underscore Singapore’s exposure to external risks despite our geographic distance from these conflict zones. Having established these risks, let us now examine how Singapore has built resilience against such challenges.

To address this vulnerability, Singapore has implemented deliberate measures to diversify its energy sources and suppliers. We import Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) from a broad range of countries—including the United States, Australia, and Qatar—thereby reducing dependence on any single region and mitigating risks associated with geopolitical disruptions. This diversification is supported by robust infrastructure: the Singapore LNG Terminal, which has a send-out capacity of 11 million tonnes per annum, alongside strategic stockpiles that provide a critical buffer against short-term supply shocks. Together, these elements enhance our ability to maintain stable energy supplies amid external uncertainties. With this foundation of resilience in place, Singapore is now turning to innovation to secure its energy future.

Looking ahead, Singapore is investing strategically in renewable and low-carbon energy sources to strengthen long-term energy security. By 2035, we aim to import up to 4 gigawatts of low-carbon electricity—expected to meet approximately 30% of domestic demand—through regional grids tapping into solar and other renewable resources. To facilitate this goal, Singapore has formalized agreements with neighboring countries such as Indonesia to enable cross-border electricity trade. Additionally, the National Hydrogen Strategy launched in 2022 targets low-carbon hydrogen as a key energy source, aiming for it to meet up to 50% of our power needs by 2050. These initiatives represent a pragmatic transition toward cleaner energy while maintaining reliability and affordability. However, it is important to recognize that energy security extends beyond infrastructure and technology—it requires active participation from all sectors of society.

Indeed, securing Singapore’s energy future involves not only government policies but also collective action from households and businesses. The residential sector accounts for approximately 14.9% of total national energy consumption—a substantial share where conservation efforts can meaningfully reduce demand. National initiatives such as Go Green SG and campaigns led by the National Environment Agency encourage households to adopt energy-saving practices that contribute directly to national resilience against supply disruptions and price fluctuations. By building awareness and promoting responsible usage across all sectors, we strengthen Singapore’s overall capacity to withstand external shocks. Energy security is therefore not solely a government responsibility; it is a shared commitment that depends on informed and conscientious behavior throughout society. Finally, let us consider how Singapore manages the complex balance between energy security, sustainability, and affordability.

Balancing reliable energy supply with environmental objectives and cost considerations presents an ongoing policy challenge for Singapore. In the 2023 World Energy Trilemma Index—a respected benchmark assessing countries on security of supply, equity (affordability), and sustainability—Singapore ranked 31st globally, reflecting both progress made and areas requiring continued attention. To align economic growth with climate commitments while maintaining competitiveness, Singapore will raise its carbon tax to S$45 per ton in 2026; this policy instrument is designed explicitly to balance environmental goals with the financial impact on businesses and consumers. Through deliberate long-term planning and adaptive policy frameworks grounded in quantitative analysis and authoritative assessment, we aim to sustain an energy system that remains secure even amid global uncertainties while supporting sustainable development for future generations. In conclusion, Singapore’s comprehensive approach—rooted in strategic diversification, innovation in low-carbon technologies, collective societal responsibility, and prudent policymaking—positions us well to navigate current challenges as well as those yet to come.

Thank you.
