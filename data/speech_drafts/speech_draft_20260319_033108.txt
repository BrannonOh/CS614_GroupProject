Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not too long ago, artificial intelligence—AI—felt like something out of a sci-fi movie, distant and abstract. But today, AI is right here with us, woven into the fabric of our daily lives. Think about how many of us use AI when planning a trip—getting personalized recommendations on where to go and what to do. Or consider how our civil service employs AI in call centres that handle conversations in all four official languages, transcribing and summarizing so officers can focus on helping you better. This isn’t the future anymore; it’s now. And as we gather at this National Day Rally, a moment for reflection and progress, we must ask: What does this mean for every Singaporean today and tomorrow?

Artificial intelligence will be a defining technology of our era. Singapore must harness it—not just to keep pace—but to raise productivity, strengthen our competitiveness, and create better opportunities for all of us.

Let’s put this into context. In 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems—it couldn’t even solve them reliably. Fast forward to 2025, AI can compete in the International Math Olympiad and perform at a gold medal level. That’s rapid progress. Back home, AI is already part of everyday life: from travel planning apps many use, to government agencies where AI transcribes calls in multiple languages and generates summaries so officers can serve you more efficiently.

So AI is no longer something distant or theoretical. The real question for Singapore is how we harness it to strengthen our economy and stay competitive in a rapidly changing world.

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise—a combination that attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs. And this isn’t just about big companies. Take Tuas Port: they’re integrating AI with robotics and autonomous machines to transform operations. At Changi Airport, they’re studying automation possibilities powered by AI. GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility here uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components—alerting human operators who then make precise diagnoses. Even SMEs are jumping on board; Q&M Dental uses AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay.

AI adoption isn’t limited to the giants—it’s an opportunity for all businesses.

But whenever new technology transforms industries, people naturally ask another important question: what will this mean for jobs?

It’s a fair question—and one we must face head-on.

History shows us that technological change causes disruption but also opens doors to better jobs. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming the economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we didn’t hesitate; we adopted it fully and propelled ourselves forward.

Look at PSA: crane operators were retrained not just to operate cranes but to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously—boosting productivity while improving job quality.

Technological shifts are never easy—they shake things up—but they also create opportunities for workers to move into higher-skilled roles with better pay.

And just as we supported workers through past transformations, we must do the same again in the age of artificial intelligence.

This time around, our approach will be people-centred.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this era. We’re expanding job matching services—with more career fairs at tertiary institutions—and launching a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils (CDCs). These efforts ensure Singaporeans have access to meaningful employment opportunities across the island.

Our SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme is also expanding: more part-time training allowance claims are now supported, along with additional courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

To give you an example: Vinoth Nanda Kumaran was 41 years old in 2025 when he transitioned from being a private hire driver to becoming a Business Intelligence Developer. He used SkillsFuture credits first to pursue a diploma in data engineering—and then continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for full-time study lasting up to 24 months.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy is one that creates good jobs and better lives for all of us—not just some of us.

Embracing AI supports Singapore’s goal of staying globally competitive—it keeps us ahead through ideas and innovation.

But more than anything else—it requires unity: working together as one people determined not just to adapt but to lead.

The future belongs to those who prepare for it today—and Singapore is ready.

Thank you.
