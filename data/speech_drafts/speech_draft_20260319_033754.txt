Good evening, fellow Singaporeans.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was something we only heard about in science fiction or distant tech reports. Today, it’s everywhere—in the apps we use to plan our holidays, recommending destinations and crafting personalised itineraries just for us. It’s in the public services we rely on daily. In our civil service call centres, AI transcribes conversations in all four official languages and generates summaries. That frees up officers to spend more time serving the public directly. This isn’t some far-off future. It’s here now, shaping how we live and work in Singapore.

Why does this matter to every one of us? Because at this National Day Rally—a moment for reflection and progress—we need to face a simple truth: artificial intelligence is no longer a distant idea; it’s a defining technology of our era. In 2020, AI struggled with basic math problems reliably. By 2025, it’s competing—and winning gold medal-level awards—at the International Math Olympiad. That rapid leap signals how fast this technology is advancing and why it demands our attention.

So the real question for Singapore is this: how do we harness AI to strengthen our economy and keep Singapore competitive in a world that’s changing faster than ever?

Artificial intelligence will shape productivity, competitiveness, and opportunities for every Singaporean. But we have to be clear-eyed about what that means.

Look at Tuas Port. There, AI combines with robotics and autonomous machines to streamline operations—making the port more efficient and competitive on the global stage. At Changi Airport, AI-driven automation is being studied to improve processes and passenger experiences. Even GE Vernova’s turbine repair facility uses AI to detect anomalies in turbine components—alerting human operators who then apply their expertise for precise diagnosis. This partnership between machine precision and human skill is exactly what makes Singapore strong.

And don’t think this is just about big companies. Small and medium enterprises are jumping on board too. Take Q&M Dental—they use AI to analyse dental X-rays more accurately, spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and tooth decay that might be missed otherwise. This helps dentists make better diagnoses and improves care for patients.

Singapore’s strength lies in combining technology with human expertise—that mix attracts high-quality investments and creates good jobs with real career paths.

But whenever new technology shakes up industries, people ask: what will this mean for jobs?

Let’s not shy away from that question. Technological change always disrupts existing roles. But history tells us something important: new technology doesn’t just take jobs away—it creates new ones that are better skilled and better paid.

Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy. When the internet arrived in the 1990s, we adopted it quickly—and that propelled us forward.

Today at PSA, crane operators have been retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once—boosting productivity while improving job quality by making work safer and less physically demanding.

We supported workers through those past transformations—and we must do the same again now with artificial intelligence.

Our approach will be people-centred from start to finish.

The government is working closely with NTUC and unions to redesign jobs so workers can thrive alongside new technologies—not be left behind.

We’re expanding job matching services across the board: more career fairs at tertiary institutions; a new town-level job matching initiative led by Community Development Councils—all designed to help Singaporeans find jobs that fit their skills and aspirations.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme will grow as well—offering more part-time training allowances; adding courses from industry leaders and private training providers.

This programme already provides Singaporeans aged 40 and above with $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to 24 months of full-time study.

Take Vinoth Nanda Kumaran as an example: at 41 years old in 2025, he transitioned from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits to earn a diploma in data engineering—and continued his studies in Business Analytics at SUSS.

His story shows what happens when support meets opportunity—the SkillsFuture programme made that transition possible.

Ultimately, these efforts are about something bigger: building an economy that remains dynamic, innovative, and full of opportunity for every Singaporean.

A dynamic economy creates good jobs—and better lives—for all of us. Embracing AI isn’t just about keeping up; it’s about staying ahead globally by focusing on ideas, innovation—and yes—unity as a nation.

Some might say AI will leave people behind or make us dependent on machines. I say that’s not inevitable—it depends on choices we make together now.

Singapore aims not just to keep pace but to lead through hard work, smart decisions, and standing united as one people.

Thank you.
