Good evening, everyone.

Here’s something to chew on: AI has exploded in capability—and it’s not some distant future anymore. Just in 2025, AI models from Google DeepMind and OpenAI scored gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s a leap from struggling with basic tasks just a few years back to mastering some of the toughest problems out there.

And right here in Singapore, AI is already woven into our daily lives. Take GovTech’s ‘Transcribe’ AI—it listens in on civil service call centre conversations across all four official languages, plus Singlish, and spits out summaries so officers can spend more time helping you directly. Planning your next trip? AI is behind those personalised travel recommendations and tailored itineraries making your holiday planning smoother and smarter.

AI has gone from fumbling simple tasks to becoming an everyday tool that Singaporeans rely on—travel, government services, and more. It’s no longer science fiction; it’s practical and real.

Having seen AI’s rapid rise and how it touches our lives now, let’s dive into how Singapore is harnessing this technology to boost productivity and create real value.

We’re not just adopting AI—we’re combining it with robotics and autonomous machines to transform entire industries. Look at Tuas Port: over 200 driverless electric vehicles move containers efficiently around the port without human drivers behind the wheel. Changi Airport is stepping up too, using AI for airside automation with autonomous tractors handling operations that used to need manual effort.

But don’t think this only benefits big players. SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyze dental X-rays with greater accuracy—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay faster than ever before. This means better diagnoses, improved patient care, and smarter resource use for smaller businesses too.

Investors see this potential clearly. In the 12 months leading up to June 2025, 495 Singapore-based AI start-ups raised a whopping US$1.31 billion—the highest funding haul in Southeast Asia by far.

Singapore’s blend of cutting-edge tech and skilled people isn’t just attracting investments; it’s creating good jobs across the board—from large firms right down to SMEs.

Now, I know there are concerns about job losses as AI advances. Let’s confront that head-on by looking at how Singapore has handled tech shifts before.

Technology shakes things up—that’s unavoidable. But history shows us what comes next. Back in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that armed students and workers with computer skills—transforming our economy fundamentally.

Then in the 1990s under the IT2000 masterplan, we rolled out Singapore ONE in 1998—the world’s first nationwide broadband network—putting us ahead of many countries in connectivity.

At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and driverless vehicles simultaneously. That move didn’t just boost productivity significantly—it also improved job quality by upgrading skills and responsibilities.

Technological change disrupts—but it also opens doors for workers to step into higher-skilled, better-paying roles. The same will happen with AI if we manage it right.

Building on that legacy, Singapore is putting its people front and centre to thrive in the AI era through targeted skills development and job redesign.

The government works closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this new world of work.

Take the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme: for Singaporeans aged 40 and above, it offers $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances up to $3,000 per month for as long as 24 months if studying full-time. That’s serious support for mid-career transitions—not just lip service.

Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is a perfect example of this in action. At 41 in 2025, he switched from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS. That’s how you turn disruption into opportunity.

On the industry side, SBS Transit tapped an NTUC Company Training Committee Grant to roll out AI-powered maintenance systems that empower workers with data-driven skills while boosting efficiency on the ground.

Job matching services are also expanding—platforms like the AI-powered MyCareersFuture portal connect people with opportunities faster than ever before. Plus, NTUC's e2i runs career fairs focused on tech jobs across sectors—making sure no one gets left behind as the economy evolves.

And SkillsFuture Level-Up itself is growing—more training options mean more chances for every Singaporean to stay relevant as technology changes fast.

With these efforts underway, Singapore isn’t just reacting—we’re ready to embrace AI head-on for a dynamic and innovative future.

Looking ahead: adopting AI could bring economic benefits of up to S$198.3 billion—or about US$147.6 billion—by 2030 here in Singapore alone (source: Access Partnership Economic Impact Report).

A dynamic economy means good jobs—and better lives—for all Singaporeans—not just some of us but everyone who calls this place home.

Some still question whether embracing AI is worth it or worry about losing control. I say: can we afford not to? Falling behind means fewer jobs and less prosperity—not what any of us want.

Singapore aims not just to keep up but stay ahead—through solid ideas that deliver results; innovation grounded in reality; and unity that binds us together as we face these changes side by side.

This isn’t about slogans or empty promises—it’s about real actions for real people in a changing world.

Thank you.
