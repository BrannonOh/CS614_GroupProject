Good evening, everyone.

Let’s start with a simple observation: AI has exploded onto the scene faster than most of us expected. Just look around Singapore today. AI isn’t some distant future concept—it’s already part of our daily lives. In 2025, AI models from Google DeepMind and OpenAI scored gold medals at the International Mathematical Olympiad. That’s not just impressive; it’s a game changer. Meanwhile, right here at home, GovTech’s ‘Transcribe’ AI is hard at work in our civil service call centres. It listens to conversations in all four official languages—and even Singlish—then generates summaries so officers can focus on helping people better. And if you’ve ever planned a trip recently, chances are AI helped you pick your destination and tailor your itinerary.

AI has come a long way—from fumbling basic tasks to mastering complex ones in just a few years. It’s no longer a novelty; it’s practical, useful, and already woven into how Singaporeans live and work.

Having seen AI’s rapid rise and current role, let’s explore how Singapore harnesses AI to boost productivity and create value.

Singapore is not just adopting AI; we’re combining it with robotics and automation to transform entire industries. Take Tuas Port—over 200 driverless electric vehicles operate there, moving containers efficiently without human drivers. Changi Airport is stepping up too, using AI for airside automation with autonomous tractors handling operations that used to require manual effort.

But it’s not just the big players benefiting. SMEs like Q&M Dental are using AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—detecting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay faster than before. This means better care for patients and smarter use of resources.

And investors see this too. In the 12 months leading to June 2025, 495 Singapore-based AI start-ups raised US$1.31 billion—the highest in Southeast Asia by far.

Singapore’s mix of tech and human skills is attracting quality investments and creating good jobs across the board—not just for big firms but for smaller businesses as well.

While AI drives growth, concerns about job displacement remain; let’s address these and Singapore’s history of adapting to change.

Yes, technology disrupts jobs—that’s nothing new. But look back: in the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills—and transformed our economy. In the 1990s under the IT2000 masterplan, we rolled out Singapore ONE in 1998—the world’s first nationwide broadband network—putting us ahead in connectivity.

At PSA, crane operators didn’t lose their jobs; they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles simultaneously. Productivity went up—and so did job quality.

History shows us that while tech change shakes things up, it also opens doors for workers to move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

Building on this legacy, Singapore is prioritising its people to thrive in the AI era through skills development and job redesign.

The government isn’t sitting back. We’re working closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this AI-driven world.

Take the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme: it gives Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to two years if they study full-time. That’s serious support for mid-career transitions.

Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is one example among many. At 41 in 2025, he switched from being a private hire driver to a Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS.

On the industry side, SBS Transit is using an NTUC Company Training Committee Grant to implement AI-powered maintenance systems that empower workers with data-driven skills while boosting efficiency.

Job matching services are also expanding—with platforms like the AI-powered MyCareersFuture portal helping connect people with opportunities faster—and career fairs run by NTUC's e2i focusing on tech jobs across sectors.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme itself is growing—more training opportunities mean more chances for Singaporeans to stay relevant as technology evolves.

With these efforts underway, Singapore is ready to embrace AI for a dynamic and innovative future.

Looking ahead, adopting AI could add up to S$198.3 billion—or US$147.6 billion—in economic benefits by 2030 here in Singapore alone.

A dynamic economy means good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just some of us but everyone who calls this place home.

Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay competitive on the global stage—it’s essential.

Singapore aims to remain ahead through ideas that work, innovation that delivers results, and unity that keeps us moving forward together.

Thank you.
