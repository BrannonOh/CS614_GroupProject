Good evening, everyone.

Let’s start with a simple observation: AI has evolved at lightning speed. Just a few years ago, AI struggled with basic tasks. Today, it’s hitting gold medal-level scores at the International Mathematical Olympiad—Google DeepMind and OpenAI models did that in 2025. That’s not some far-off future; it’s happening now.

Right here in Singapore, AI is already part of daily life. GovTech’s ‘Transcribe’ AI listens in on civil service call centre conversations—in all four official languages and even Singlish—and creates summaries so officers can focus on helping people better. And if you’ve planned a trip recently, chances are AI helped you pick your destination and tailor your itinerary.

AI has moved fast—from fumbling simple things to mastering complex ones in just a few years. It’s no longer an experiment; it’s practical and useful for Singaporeans every day.

Having seen AI’s rapid rise and current role, let’s explore how Singapore harnesses AI to boost productivity and create value.

Singapore isn’t just adopting AI; we’re combining it with robotics and automation to transform industries. At Tuas Port, over 200 driverless electric vehicles move containers efficiently without human drivers. Changi Airport is using AI for airside automation, deploying autonomous tractors to handle operations once done manually.

But it’s not only big firms benefiting. SMEs like Q&M Dental use AI to analyze dental X-rays more accurately—spotting fillings, impacted teeth, and decay faster than before. That means better care and smarter use of resources.

Investors notice this too. In the 12 months to June 2025, 495 Singapore-based AI start-ups raised US$1.31 billion—the highest in Southeast Asia by far.

Singapore’s mix of technology and human expertise attracts quality investments and creates good jobs—not just for large companies but also for smaller businesses.

While AI drives growth, concerns about job displacement remain. Let’s address these concerns by looking at how Singapore has adapted to past technological shifts.

Technology disrupts jobs—that’s a fact we can’t ignore. But history shows us what happens next. In the 1980s, Singapore launched a nationwide computerisation drive that equipped students and workers with computer skills, transforming the economy.

In the 1990s under the IT2000 masterplan, we rolled out Singapore ONE in 1998—the world’s first nationwide broadband network—putting us ahead in connectivity.

At PSA, crane operators weren’t replaced; they were retrained to remotely supervise multiple cranes and vehicles at once. Productivity went up—and so did job quality.

So yes, technological change shakes things up. But it also opens doors for workers to move into higher-skilled, better-paying roles.

Building on this legacy, Singapore is prioritising its people to thrive in the AI era through skills development and job redesign.

The government is working closely with unions to redesign jobs and equip workers with new skills tailored for this AI-driven world.

Take the SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme: it gives Singaporeans aged 40 and above $4,000 in SkillsFuture credits plus training allowances of up to $3,000 per month for up to two years if they study full-time. That’s serious support for mid-career transitions.

Vinoth Nanda Kumaran is one example among many. At 41 in 2025, he switched from private hire driver to Business Intelligence Developer after using SkillsFuture credits for a diploma in data engineering—and he didn’t stop there; he continued studying Business Analytics at SUSS.

On the industry side, SBS Transit is using an NTUC Company Training Committee Grant to implement AI-powered maintenance systems that empower workers with data-driven skills while boosting efficiency.

Job matching services are also expanding—with platforms like the AI-powered MyCareersFuture portal connecting people with opportunities faster—and career fairs run by NTUC's e2i focusing on tech jobs across sectors.

The SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme itself is growing—more training opportunities mean more chances for Singaporeans to stay relevant as technology evolves.

With these efforts underway, Singapore is ready to embrace AI for a dynamic and innovative future.

Looking ahead, adopting AI could add up to S$198.3 billion—or US$147.6 billion—in economic benefits by 2030 here in Singapore alone.

A dynamic economy means good jobs and better lives for all Singaporeans—not just some of us but everyone who calls this place home.

Embracing AI isn’t optional if we want to stay competitive globally—it’s necessary.

Singapore aims to remain ahead through ideas that work, innovation that delivers results, and unity that keeps us moving forward together.

Thank you.
